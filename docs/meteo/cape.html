Storm fuel: CAPE, CIN, and the key levels

When you call analyze() on a sounding (or probe() a live column), you get back a SoundingReportData. This page decodes the thermodynamic half of that report - the numbers that answer “will this air storm, and how hard?” The wind half (shear, helicity, storm motion) is the next chapter.

All of these come from one thought experiment: lift a parcel of surface air straight up and watch how it compares to its surroundings at each height. That is the story from Why air rises, now turned into numbers.

CAPE - the fuel gauge

CAPE stands for Convective Available Potential Energy. It is the total energy a rising parcel gains from buoyancy on its way up - the size of the storm’s fuel tank. Units are joules per kilogram (J/kg), and it is the field report.cape.

Bigger CAPE means the updraft can accelerate harder and reach higher, which means stronger storms, taller clouds, bigger hail, heavier rain.

ELI5: CAPE is how much “push up” the atmosphere has stored for a parcel that gets going. Small CAPE is a gentle nudge. Huge CAPE is a trampoline.

A rough scale to calibrate your soundings:

CAPE (J/kg)What to expect
0No convection possible. Parcel never becomes buoyant.
100 - 1000Weak - small showers, fair-weather cumulus.
1000 - 2500Moderate - healthy thunderstorms.
2500 - 4000Strong - severe storm potential.
4000+Extreme - explosive updrafts.

The engine’s sounding_convective() sits comfortably in the storm-producing range. If your custom sounding reports CAPE near zero, it will not storm no matter how hard you seed it - the fix is a warmer or (more often) moister low level, which raises CAPE.

CIN - the lid you must break

CIN stands for Convective Inhibition. It is the energy the parcel loses in the stable layer near the ground before it reaches the height where it becomes buoyant. It is the strength of the “cap” or “lid” holding the storm down. Units are J/kg; the field report.cin is reported as a positive magnitude (a bigger number = a stronger cap).

CIN is the barrier from the ball-in-a-dip analogy. Your seed (or feed, or air-mass boundary) has to supply enough lift to carry the parcel over it.

  • Low CIN (near 0) - almost no cap. Storms fire easily, even from a small seed. Can be too easy: everything erupts at once.
  • Moderate CIN - a cap that suppresses weak triggers but breaks under a strong one. This is the sweet spot: it lets pressure build so that when a storm goes, it goes big and isolated (the classic supercell setup).
  • High CIN - a strong cap. Even a good seed may fail to break through. If your storms fizzle immediately, high CIN is a prime suspect.

There is a real tension here: CAPE wants to be high (more fuel) and CIN wants to be low enough that your trigger can break it. High CAPE with a cap that never breaks produces nothing. Tuning a good storm environment is largely balancing these two.

The three key levels

As the parcel rises it passes three named heights, all reported in meters. Any of them can be NaN (not-a-number) if it does not exist for that sounding - for example, a parcel that never becomes buoyant has no LFC or EL. Always check for NaN before using them.

LCL - lifting condensation level (report.lcl)

The height where the rising parcel cools to saturation and cloud forms - literally cloud base. A low LCL means moist air and clouds that sit close to the ground; a high LCL means dry air and high, ragged cloud bases. Low LCL is one ingredient in tornado-friendly setups (the rotation has less distance to reach the ground).

LFC - level of free convection (report.lfc)

The height where the parcel finally becomes warmer than its surroundings and rises on its own, no more lift needed. This is the “over the lip of the dip” point - above it, the storm is self-sustaining. The gap between the surface and the LFC is the capped layer whose energy cost is CIN.

EL - equilibrium level (report.el)

High up, where the parcel has cooled back to match its surroundings and stops accelerating. This is roughly the storm top - the height of the flat anvil cloud. A high EL means a deep storm. It also tells you how tall your simulation domain needs to be: if your domain top is below the EL, you are clipping the storm.

Here is the vertical picture:

  EL   ~ 12 km  --- storm top / anvil; parcel stops rising
       |
       |         <- parcel buoyant here; energy gained = CAPE
  LFC  ~ 2 km   --- becomes buoyant on its own ("over the lip")
       |         <- capped layer; energy lost = CIN
  LCL  ~ 1 km   --- cloud base; vapor starts condensing
       |         <- parcel lifted by your seed, still not buoyant
 sfc     0 m    --- surface parcel starts here

Reading the report in code

SoundingReportData r = sounding->analyze();
if (!r.valid) { /* probe fell outside the domain; ignore */ }

std::printf("CAPE %.0f J/kg   CIN %.0f J/kg\n", r.cape, r.cin);
std::printf("cloud base (LCL) %.0f m\n", r.lcl);
if (!std::isnan(r.lfc)) std::printf("self-sustaining above (LFC) %.0f m\n", r.lfc);
if (!std::isnan(r.el))  std::printf("storm top (EL) %.0f m\n", r.el);

The same SoundingReportData shape comes back three ways, always meaning the same thing:

  • from analyze() on a sounding you authored (before running),
  • from probe(position) on a live simulation column (an Probed event) - the effective sounding right now at a spot, which changes as the storm modifies its own environment,
  • and its two headline composite indices, supercell_composite and significant_tornado, combine this thermodynamic fuel with the wind ingredients from the next chapter.

A tuning checklist

When an environment is not behaving, read the report in this order:

  1. CAPE near zero? No fuel. Moisten the low levels or warm the surface.
  2. CAPE fine but nothing fires? The cap (CIN) is too strong for your seed. Seed harder (bigger warm/moist amplitude), or soften the cap.
  3. Everything erupts instantly and messily? CIN is too low. Add a little cap so storms stay isolated.
  4. Storm looks chopped off at the top? Your domain top is below the EL. Raise the domain (taller vertical axis).

Takeaways

  • CAPE (J/kg) is the fuel: how much energy a rising parcel gains. Higher = stronger storms. Aim for ~1000+ to storm, ~2500+ for severe.
  • CIN (J/kg) is the cap: energy the parcel must be forced through first. Some CIN is good (isolated storms); too much means your seed cannot break it.
  • LCL / LFC / EL are cloud base, the self-sustaining height, and the storm top, in meters. They can be NaN - check before use. The EL also tells you how tall your domain must be.
  • The same report comes from analyze() (before) and probe() (live), so you can tune offline and monitor online.
