WeatherEngine - Game-ready weather simulations

Introduction

WeatherEngine grows real weather inside your game. You describe a patch of sky - how warm it is, how moist, which way the wind blows - drop in a warm bubble of air, and the engine does the rest: the bubble rises, a cloud forms, rain falls, and if you set the stage right, a rotating storm spins up. Nothing is scripted. The clouds are where the physics puts them.

This book has two jobs:

  1. Teach you the weather. WeatherEngine speaks the language of meteorology - the science of weather. Its functions ask for things like “potential temperature”, “mixing ratio”, and “CAPE”. If those words mean nothing to you yet, that is fine. This book starts from zero. By the end of Part II you will read a weather report the way the engine does.
  2. Connect the weather to the code. Every concept ends by pointing at the exact struct, field, or function that uses it, so you always know why an input exists and what number to put there.

Who this is for

You are building a game (Unreal or any C++ engine) and you want weather that behaves like weather, not a looping particle effect. You are comfortable with C++. You may know little or no meteorology - this book assumes none.

If you already know your CAPE from your CIN, skim Parts I and II and jump to Part III, where the simulation itself begins.

What WeatherEngine is (and is not)

WeatherEngine is a small, focused layer over a physics core (codenamed “anvil”). It simulates a box of atmosphere on a grid and lets your game read the results each frame. Think of it as a weather actor: you send it commands, it runs the storm on a background thread, and it sends you back data to render.

It is not a forecast system, a global climate model, or a full cloud of particles you hand-place. You do not paint every raindrop. You set up the environment and the trigger, and the storm is an emergent result.

Here is the whole loop, in spirit:

// 1. Describe the air (an atmospheric profile).
Box<Sounding> sounding = sounding_convective();

// 2. Spawn the simulation worker and build a world for it.
Box<SimClient> client = new_sim_client(0);
client->create_sim(config, axis, std::move(sounding), options);

// 3. Kick off a storm: seed a warm, moist bubble.
Box<Scenario> scenario = new_scenario();
scenario->add_seed_ellipsoid(Field::Theta, center, radii, 3.0f);
client->install_scenario(std::move(scenario));

// 4. Each frame: step the weather, then read it out.
client->advance(5, 5);
client->grab_presets(view, presets);
// ... drain events, upload the cloud volume to the renderer ...

Every line above is explained later. For now, notice the shape: describe, build, trigger, step-and-read. That is the entire mental model.

How to read this book

  • Part I builds the ideas of weather from nothing: heat, water, wind, and why air moves up and down. This is the ELI5 (“explain like I’m five”) foundation. If a term is new, it is defined the first time it appears.
  • Part II puts those ideas together into a sounding - a vertical slice through the atmosphere - and shows how the engine reads storm potential out of it (CAPE, shear, and friends).
  • Part III is the engine itself: how it runs on a background thread, how you build a world, start the weather, keep the grid focused on the storm, and pull data out to render.
  • Part IV is quick reference: ready-made recipes and a glossary.

A note on units: WeatherEngine is metric throughout. Distances are in meters, time in seconds, temperature in kelvin (K), and wind in meters per second (m/s). One meter per second is about 3.6 km/h or 2.2 mph. We will remind you as these come up.

Ready? Let us start with what the air actually is.

The atmosphere in five minutes

Before we can make weather, we need a shared picture of what weather is. This page is the whole atmosphere, stripped down to the parts the engine cares about. Everything later builds on these five ideas.

Air is a fluid, and it has weight

Air feels like nothing, but it is a real substance - a mix of gases (mostly nitrogen and oxygen) plus some water. Like water in a pool, air is a fluid: it flows, it piles up, it pushes on things. And it has weight. A column of air from the ground to space presses down on you with about the weight of a small car spread over every square meter.

That downward push is pressure. Near the ground there is a lot of air stacked above you, so pressure is high. Climb a mountain and there is less air above you, so pressure drops. Pressure always falls as you go up. This one fact drives almost everything else.

ELI5: Imagine a tall stack of pillows. The bottom pillow is squished flat by all the ones on top. Air at the ground is the bottom pillow - squished by the miles of air above it. Air high up is a top pillow - almost nothing pressing on it, so it puffs out thin.

In WeatherEngine, pressure is the Pressure field, measured in pascals (Pa). The engine actually tracks the small departure from the resting pressure (the “perturbation”), because that tiny difference is what pushes air around. More on that in Wind.

Warm air is light, cold air is heavy

Heat makes air expand and get thinner (less dense). Thinner air is lighter than the air around it, so it floats up - exactly like a hot-air balloon. Cold air is denser and heavier, so it sinks.

This up-and-down motion driven by warmth is called convection, and it is the engine of nearly every interesting weather event: cumulus clouds, thunderstorms, the works. We give convection its own page (Why air rises) because it is that important.

The air holds invisible water

There is always water vapor in the air - water in gas form, invisible, mixed in with everything else. Warm air can hold a lot of vapor; cold air can hold very little. When moist air is cooled enough, the vapor it can no longer hold turns back into tiny liquid droplets. That is a cloud. Cool it more and the droplets clump into drops heavy enough to fall - that is rain.

So the life of water in the sky is a three-step ladder the engine models directly:

  • Vapor - invisible gas (the Vapor field)
  • Cloud - tiny floating droplets (the Cloud field)
  • Rain - drops heavy enough to fall (the Rain field)

We follow this ladder in detail in Water in the air.

The air is layered, and it thins with height

The weather all happens in the bottom layer of the atmosphere, called the troposphere - from the ground up to about 10-12 km. That is where the clouds, storms, and wind you care about live. Above it the air is stable and calm (the stratosphere), which acts like a lid that storm tops splat against (you have seen the flat “anvil” top of a thunderstorm - that is the lid at work).

Because so much changes with height, weather is really a vertical story. To describe the air over one spot you cannot use a single temperature; you need temperature at every height. That stack of values by height is called a sounding, and it is so central that it gets its own page (The sounding) and is the first thing you hand the engine.

Wind is just air moving

Wind is air flowing from where pressure is high to where it is low, bent and stirred by the spinning Earth and by the ups and downs of convection. Wind has direction and speed, and - importantly for storms - it usually changes with height: slow near the ground, faster and from a different direction higher up. That change with height is wind shear, and it is what turns a plain rain shower into an organized, rotating storm (Wind shear and rotating storms).

The engine splits wind into three numbers at every point:

  • U - wind blowing toward the east, m/s
  • V - wind blowing toward the north, m/s
  • W - wind blowing straight up, m/s

Putting it together

That is the whole show:

  1. Air is a heavy fluid, and pressure falls with height.
  2. Warm air rises, cold air sinks (convection).
  3. Air carries water that cycles vapor to cloud to rain.
  4. The air is layered and thins upward; weather is a vertical story.
  5. Wind is moving air, and it changes with height (shear).

Every WeatherEngine input is one of these ideas turned into a number. Next we make the first one precise: how the engine measures heat, and why it does not use the temperature you would read off a thermometer.

Heat and potential temperature

The engine’s most important field is temperature - but not the temperature you read off a thermometer. It uses a cousin called potential temperature, written with the Greek letter theta (θ) and exposed as the Field::Theta field. This page explains what it is and why it exists, because you cannot seed a storm without it.

First, the units: kelvin

Weather science measures temperature in kelvin (K), not Celsius or Fahrenheit. Kelvin is just Celsius shifted so that 0 K is the coldest possible temperature (absolute zero). To convert:

  • kelvin = celsius + 273.15

So water freezes at 273.15 K (0 °C) and a warm 27 °C day is about 300 K. That number - 300 K - is a good “comfortable surface air” anchor to keep in your head, because it is exactly the surface value the engine’s built-in soundings use.

The problem with plain temperature

Here is the trouble. Take a balloon of air near the ground and carry it upward without adding or removing any heat. As it rises into lower pressure, it expands, and expanding cools it down. It gets colder purely from going up - even though no heat left it.

ELI5: Squeeze a bike pump and the air inside gets hot. Let it expand and it gets cold. Air does the same thing when it moves up (expands, cools) or down (squeezes, warms). This is why mountaintops are cold: the air there has expanded.

This is a headache if you want to compare air at different heights. A parcel of air at the ground might read 300 K and the “same” air lifted to 1 km might read 290 K, but nothing was actually done to it - it only moved. Plain temperature mixes up “how much heat is in this air” with “how high up it happens to be.”

The fix: potential temperature (theta)

Potential temperature is the temperature a parcel of air would have if you brought it down to a standard reference pressure (sea-level-ish), letting it squeeze and warm on the way. It strips out the effect of height.

The payoff: if you move a parcel up or down without adding heat, its plain temperature changes but its potential temperature stays the same. So theta is a clean label for “which air is this” that does not care about altitude.

That gives us a beautifully simple rule for stability:

  • If theta is the same at all heights, the air is neutral - a lifted parcel matches its surroundings and neither rises nor sinks on its own.
  • If theta increases with height (warm-labeled air sitting on top of cooler-labeled air), the air is stable - lift a parcel and it is colder and denser than its new surroundings, so it sinks back. Calm.
  • If theta decreases with height, the air is unstable - a lifted parcel is warmer and lighter than its surroundings, so it keeps going up. This is the recipe for storms.

You will meet this rule again, made precise, in Why air rises.

How the engine uses theta

Two things to know for the code.

1. The base sounding sets theta at every height. When you build a sounding (next chapters), you give it a theta profile - theta versus altitude. The built-in sounding_quiet() uses theta rising from 300 K at the surface to 380 K at 20 km: strongly stable, so nothing happens. The built-in sounding_convective() uses a profile that is only gently stable in the middle - “conditionally unstable” - which is primed to erupt once you give it a push.

2. Seeds and feeds work in perturbations - offsets from that base. The engine does not store the full theta at each cell. It stores the small difference from the base sounding, called the perturbation (often written theta’, “theta-prime”). Zero perturbation means “exactly the background air.” A positive perturbation means “warmer than the background here.”

This is why a warm bubble looks like this:

// A +3 K warm blob: 3 kelvin warmer than the surrounding air, right here.
scenario->add_seed_ellipsoid(Field::Theta, center, radii, /*amplitude=*/3.0f);

The amplitude of 3.0f is 3 K above ambient, not 3 K absolute. Three kelvin does not sound like much, but in conditionally unstable air it is enough to make the bubble float up, and once it starts rising it feeds itself. That single warm nudge is how most storms in the engine are born.

The same perturbation idea is why the TierTrigger for the adaptive grid watches theta_above (a threshold on |theta'|, the size of the departure from background): a big theta perturbation marks where the interesting, buoyant air is.

Takeaways

  • Temperatures are in kelvin; 300 K is a comfortable surface value and the engine’s default surface anchor.
  • Plain temperature changes just from moving up or down, which makes it useless for comparing air across heights.
  • Potential temperature (theta) removes the height effect. Constant theta with height = neutral; increasing = stable; decreasing = unstable.
  • The Theta field and its seeds are perturbations - offsets from the base sounding, in kelvin. A +3.0f seed is a 3 K warm bubble.

Next: the other half of a storm’s fuel - water.

Water in the air

Water is the other half of a storm. Heat makes air rise; water is what turns that rising air into visible clouds, rain, and a huge extra kick of energy. The engine tracks water in three forms, and you author the first one directly. This page explains all three and the units they use.

Mixing ratio: how the engine measures moisture

Moisture is measured as a mixing ratio: the mass of water divided by the mass of the dry air it is mixed into. The unit is kilograms of water per kilogram of air (kg/kg).

These numbers are small. A humid near-surface value is around 0.014 kg/kg. Meteorologists often say this as grams per kilogram (g/kg) to avoid the zeros: 0.014 kg/kg is 14 g/kg. The engine’s API always uses kg/kg, so 0.014f is what you type, but “14 grams of water in every kilo of air” is the picture to hold.

ELI5: Imagine every kilogram of air is a bag of invisible marbles. Mixing ratio counts how many of those marbles are water. Fourteen out of a thousand is a moist, storm-ready bag. One or two is bone dry.

The three forms of water

The engine follows water through three stages, each its own field.

1. Vapor - invisible gas (Field::Vapor)

Water vapor is water as a gas, mixed invisibly into the air. This is the moisture you author and the fuel a storm drinks. You set it in the sounding as a vapor profile (mixing ratio versus height), and you can seed extra vapor into a region:

// A moist blob: +0.003 kg/kg (3 g/kg) of vapor above the background.
scenario->add_seed_ellipsoid(Field::Vapor, center, radii, 0.003f);

Warm air holds more vapor than cold air. There is a ceiling at any temperature called saturation - the most vapor that air can hold. Push past it and the extra has to condense.

2. Cloud - tiny floating droplets (Field::Cloud)

When rising air cools to its saturation point, vapor condenses into Cloud: microscopic liquid droplets, light enough to float. This is the visible cloud. You do not usually author cloud directly - the engine creates it wherever the physics says air got cold and moist enough. You read it back out to render (the ChannelPreset::Cloud grab preset).

The height where a rising surface parcel first hits saturation and cloud begins is the lifting condensation level (LCL) - literally cloud base. It shows up in the sounding report (CAPE and the key levels).

3. Rain - drops heavy enough to fall (Field::Rain)

When cloud droplets collide and merge into drops too heavy to float, they become Rain and fall. Falling rain drags air down with it, which is how storms produce cool downdrafts and gusty outflow at the ground. The engine accumulates what reaches the surface into a precipitation map you can query in millimeters (Getting data back out).

The hidden energy: why moisture makes storms violent

Here is the part that makes water special, not just decorative.

Turning vapor into liquid releases heat - the same heat it took to evaporate that water in the first place, now handed back. This is called latent heat (“latent” = hidden, stored).

ELI5: Sweat cools you because evaporating water takes heat from your skin. Run that backwards: when vapor turns into cloud droplets, it gives that heat back to the air around it.

So when a moist parcel rises and its vapor starts condensing into cloud, the condensation warms the parcel from the inside. Warmer means lighter, lighter means it rises faster, faster rising means more condensation - a self-feeding engine. This is why a humid day produces towering thunderstorms and a dry day produces, at most, wispy clouds. The moisture you put in the sounding is the storm’s fuel tank.

This latent-heat feedback is exactly why a tiny +3 K warm seed can grow into a full storm: the seed only has to lift the moist air high enough to start condensation, and the released latent heat takes over from there.

What you control vs. what emerges

A useful split to keep straight:

FieldYou author it?Where it comes from
VaporYes - sounding profile + optional seedsYour input; the fuel
CloudNoEmerges when rising air saturates
RainNoEmerges when cloud droplets merge and fall

You pour in vapor (and heat); the engine makes the cloud and rain. If your storms are too weak, the usual fix is a moister sounding or a moister seed - a bigger fuel tank - not more force.

Takeaways

  • Moisture is a mixing ratio in kg/kg; 0.014 kg/kg (14 g/kg) is moist, storm-ready near-surface air.
  • Water lives in three fields: Vapor (gas, you author it), Cloud (floating droplets, emerges), Rain (falling drops, emerges).
  • Condensation releases latent heat, which warms rising air and makes moist storms self-feeding and strong.
  • You supply vapor and a trigger; cloud, rain, and their energy come out of the physics.

Next: the third ingredient - wind.

Wind

Wind is moving air. It ventilates storms, carries them across the map, and - when it changes with height - gives them the twist that turns an ordinary shower into an organized, long-lived, rotating storm. This page covers how the engine describes wind and the world it lives in.

Three numbers at every point

The engine splits wind into three components, each a field measured in meters per second (m/s):

  • U - wind blowing toward the east (positive = eastward)
  • V - wind blowing toward the north (positive = northward)
  • W - wind blowing straight up (positive = upward)

U and V together are the horizontal wind - the breeze you would feel and the direction a storm drifts. W is the vertical wind, which is almost always tiny in the background air but becomes large and violent inside a storm’s updraft (rising) and downdraft (sinking). A strong thunderstorm updraft can have W of tens of meters per second.

A quick feel for the units:

  • 1 m/s ≈ 3.6 km/h ≈ 2.2 mph (a slow walk)
  • 10 m/s ≈ 36 km/h (a stiff breeze, or a typical mid-level wind)
  • 30 m/s ≈ 108 km/h (a violent updraft core, or a gale)

The world’s axes

The engine’s world is a box. Horizontal position is a pair of meters, Vec2{x, y}, where x is east and y is north, matching U and V. Height is a third axis, z, pointing up, matching W. A full 3D point is Vec3{x, y, z} in meters.

So when you place a seed at Vec3{0.0, 0.0, 1000.0}, you mean “centered horizontally at the origin, 1000 meters above the ground.” When you read a storm-motion vector of Vec2{9.0, 5.0} m/s, you mean “moving 9 m/s east and 5 m/s north.”

Background wind: authored in the sounding

The starting wind of your world - the calm, large-scale flow before any storm - is part of the sounding, given as a wind profile: an east/north wind at each height.

// Wind that turns and strengthens with height (altitude m, east m/s, north m/s).
WindPoint wind[] = {
    {   0.0f, -8.0f, -6.0f},   // near the ground: from the southeast, gentle
    {2000.0f, -2.0f, -1.0f},
    {6000.0f,  8.0f,  1.0f},   // mid levels: from the west, stronger
    {12000.0f, 12.0f, 2.0f},   // near the storm top: strong westerly
};
sounding->set_wind(Slice<const WindPoint>{wind, 4});

Notice how the wind both speeds up and changes direction as you go up. That is not decoration - it is the single most important control over what kind of storm you get. The next section says why.

Wind shear: the change of wind with height

Wind shear is how much the wind changes as you climb. If the wind is 5 m/s from the south at the ground and 25 m/s from the west at 6 km, there is strong shear between those levels - a big change in both speed and direction.

Shear matters because of what it does to a storm’s structure:

  • Low shear (wind about the same at all heights): a storm’s rising warm air and its falling cooled rain end up in the same place. The rain falls back into the updraft and chokes it. You get brief, disorganized “popcorn” showers that rain themselves out in 20 minutes.
  • High shear (wind changes a lot with height): the upper winds blow the rain and downdraft away from the updraft, so the two do not fight. The storm can stand up straight, breathe, and live for hours. High shear also tilts and stretches the flow into a rotating column - the seed of a supercell, the storm type that makes large hail and tornadoes.

The engine measures shear for you as the 0-6 km bulk shear vector: the difference between the wind near 6 km and the wind near the ground. It comes back in the sounding report as shear_0_6km. As a rough guide, a bulk shear magnitude above about 20 m/s favors organized, supercell-type storms.

There is a subtler, twist-related cousin of shear called helicity that governs whether a storm actually rotates. It needs its own discussion, so it waits for Wind shear and rotating storms.

Why wind can carry a storm off your grid

A storm drifts with the wind. If your background wind is 12 m/s and you simulate for ten minutes, the storm travels about 7 km. If your fine grid or your domain is only a few km wide, the storm walks right off it.

The engine gives you two tools for this, both introduced later:

  • Park the storm. A supercell tends to move at a predictable speed and direction (the Bunkers storm motion, from the sounding report). Subtract that motion from your whole wind profile and the storm stands still in the box while the world flows past it. See rotating storms and the recipe for a parked supercell.
  • Let the fine grid chase it. The adaptive grid (the regridder) keeps high resolution wherever the storm is and lets it go coarse elsewhere, so the fine cells follow the storm across the domain. See Following the storm.

Takeaways

  • Wind is three fields in m/s: U (east), V (north), W (up). Horizontal U/V drift the storm; vertical W is the updraft.
  • The world uses x=east, y=north, z=up, all in meters.
  • Background wind is authored in the sounding as an east/north profile by height.
  • Wind shear - the change of wind with height - decides whether you get a brief shower (low shear) or an organized, rotating supercell (high shear). The engine reports 0-6 km bulk shear for you.
  • Wind carries storms; you can park the storm or let the fine grid chase it.

Next, the idea we keep leaning on: why air rises in the first place.

Why air rises: stability and convection

This is the heart of the whole thing. Every cloud and storm the engine makes begins with a lump of air deciding to go up. Whether it goes up, how far, and how violently, is set by two ideas: buoyancy and stability. Get these and the rest of the book is bookkeeping.

Buoyancy: the hot-air-balloon rule

A parcel of air floats or sinks by comparing its density to the air right around it - exactly like a cork in water.

  • Warmer than its surroundings -> lighter -> it rises (positive buoyancy).
  • Colder than its surroundings -> heavier -> it sinks (negative buoyancy).
  • Same as its surroundings -> it just sits there (neutral).

ELI5: A hot-air balloon rises because the air in the balloon is warmer and lighter than the air outside. Cool that air and the balloon drops. Every cloud is a hot-air balloon made of air that got warmer than its neighbors.

Two things make a parcel lighter than its neighbors: it is warmer (higher theta), or it is moister (water vapor is actually lighter than dry air, and - much bigger - condensing vapor releases latent heat that warms the parcel, from Water in the air). Warm plus moist is the buoyant, storm-making combination, which is why the engine’s classic trigger seeds both theta and vapor.

Stability: does the atmosphere fight lifting or help it?

Buoyancy is about one parcel. Stability is about the whole column: if you nudge a parcel up a little, does it come back, or does it run away?

Recall the theta rule from Heat and potential temperature:

  • Stable - theta increases with height. Lift a parcel and it is now colder than its new surroundings, so it sinks back. The atmosphere resists motion. Calm skies. (The engine’s sounding_quiet() is strongly stable - weather refuses to happen in it.)
  • Neutral - theta constant with height. A lifted parcel matches its surroundings and neither rises nor sinks.
  • Unstable - theta decreases with height. A lifted parcel is warmer than its surroundings and keeps accelerating up. Runaway. Storms.

Pure instability is rare in the real world, because the sun heats the ground and the resulting convection quickly mixes it away. The interesting case - the case that makes thunderstorms - is in between.

Conditional instability: the loaded gun

Most storm days are conditionally unstable. That means:

  • For a dry parcel, the column is stable - nudge it up and it sinks back.
  • But for a moist parcel that rises far enough to start condensing, the latent-heat kick makes it buoyant, and then it runs away.

The “condition” is: get the moist air high enough to start condensing, and it takes off on its own. Below that height the atmosphere holds it down; above it, the atmosphere lets it go.

ELI5: A ball sitting in a small dip at the top of a hill. Give it a tiny push and it rolls back into the dip - stable. But push it just over the lip of the dip and it rolls all the way down the hill - unstable. The lip is the barrier; the moist air only needs one good shove past it.

This is exactly the environment sounding_convective() sets up, and it is why you fire a storm with a small warm, moist seed. The seed is the “shove.” It does not need to be strong; it only needs to lift the moist boundary-layer air past its barrier.

The three barriers and levels

When you lift a surface parcel in a conditionally unstable atmosphere, it passes through named milestones. These are the numbers the sounding report gives you, so meet them here as physical heights and get the full treatment in CAPE and the key levels:

  1. LCL - lifting condensation level. The height where the rising parcel cools to saturation and cloud forms. This is cloud base.
  2. LFC - level of free convection. The height where the parcel finally becomes warmer than its surroundings and starts rising on its own, no more push needed. This is the “over the lip” point.
  3. EL - equilibrium level. High up, where the parcel has cooled back to match its surroundings and stops accelerating. This is roughly the storm top - the flat anvil.

Between the surface and the LFC there is often a warm stable layer the parcel must be forced through - the cap or lid. The energy cost of punching through it is CIN (convective inhibition). Above the LFC, the parcel gains energy all the way to the EL - and the total gain is CAPE (convective available potential energy), the storm’s fuel gauge. Both are next chapter.

From one bubble to a storm: the convective loop

Put the pieces together and you get the self-sustaining cycle that is a thunderstorm:

  1. Something lifts moist surface air past the cap (your seed, or a feed, or an air-mass boundary).
  2. Above the LFC the parcel is buoyant and accelerates upward - an updraft (large positive W).
  3. Rising cools the air to saturation; vapor condenses into cloud, releasing latent heat that makes the parcel even more buoyant. The updraft strengthens. This is the feedback that does the real work.
  4. Higher up, cloud droplets merge into rain that grows too heavy to hold. It falls, dragging cool air down as a downdraft (negative W).
  5. The cool downdraft spreads out at the ground as gusty outflow, which can lift more warm moist air at its leading edge - sometimes firing new cells. In sheared wind, the updraft and downdraft stay separated and the whole system organizes and persists.

The engine simulates all of this from first principles. You never script the updraft; you set the stage (a conditionally unstable, moist, sheared sounding) and light the fuse (a seed), and the loop above runs itself.

How this maps to the engine

  • Stability lives in the sounding’s theta and vapor profiles. Author a strongly stable one (sounding_quiet) for calm; a conditionally unstable one (sounding_convective) for storms.
  • The trigger is a Field::Theta (and usually Field::Vapor) seed - a small warm, moist bubble that provides the initial lift.
  • The updraft shows up as large W, and it is what the adaptive grid watches: the regridder’s w_above trigger refines the grid wherever the updraft is strong, keeping resolution on the storm.
  • CAPE, CIN, LCL, LFC, EL are all reported by analyze() and by probe(), so you can check before stepping whether your sounding will actually storm.

Takeaways

  • Air rises when it is warmer or moister (lighter) than its surroundings
    • buoyancy, the hot-air-balloon rule.
  • Stability is the column’s response to lifting: stable air pushes a parcel back, unstable air lets it run. Theta increasing with height = stable; decreasing = unstable.
  • Storm days are conditionally unstable: stable until moist air is lifted past a barrier, then explosively unstable. That is why a small warm, moist seed can grow a whole storm.
  • A thunderstorm is a self-feeding loop: lift -> updraft -> condensation releases heat -> stronger updraft -> rain -> downdraft -> more lift.

You now understand weather. Part II turns this into the numbers the engine reads, starting with the object that holds them all: the sounding.

The sounding

A sounding is a vertical slice through the atmosphere over one spot: the temperature, moisture, and wind at every height, from the ground up. It is the single most important object in WeatherEngine, because it is your world’s starting atmosphere. Before you can run any weather, you hand the engine a sounding.

The name comes from weather balloons, which “sound” the atmosphere by rising through it and radioing back readings as they climb.

What a sounding contains

Three profiles, each a list of (altitude, value) points from the ground up, plus one surface number:

ProfileWhat it isFieldUnits
Thetapotential temperature vs. heightset_thetaK
Vaporwater vapor mixing ratio vs. heightset_vaporkg/kg
Windeast/north wind vs. heightset_windm/s
Surface pressurepressure at the groundset_surface_pressurePa

Together these decide everything about whether and how your world storms: the theta profile sets stability, the vapor profile sets the fuel, and the wind profile sets shear (and therefore storm organization and rotation).

The Sounding handle

A Sounding is a small, pure, synchronous object. You build and inspect it right on the game thread - it does no simulation and costs almost nothing. The engine gives you three ready-made starting points:

Box<Sounding> a = sounding_new();          // neutral, does nothing
Box<Sounding> b = sounding_quiet();        // stable, calm reference
Box<Sounding> c = sounding_convective();   // storm-ready environment
  • sounding_new() - a blank, neutral atmosphere. A starting point you fully overwrite with your own profiles.
  • sounding_quiet() - a stable, slightly moist, calm atmosphere. Weather refuses to happen in it. Use it as a background or a control.
  • sounding_convective() - a storm-ready environment based on the classic Weisman-Klemp analytic sounding used in storm research: conditionally unstable up to a 12 km tropopause, a moist boundary layer, and wind that veers and strengthens with height. This is the one you seed a bubble into to make a thunderstorm. Start here.

Authoring your own profiles

To build a custom atmosphere, set each profile from an array of points (altitudes must ascend). Values between your points are interpolated, and above your top point the last value holds.

Box<Sounding> sounding = sounding_new();

// Potential temperature: gently stable low down (a storm profile).
ProfilePoint theta[] = {
    {    0.0f, 300.0f},
    {12000.0f, 343.0f},   // tropopause
    {20000.0f, 380.0f},   // stable stratosphere above
};
sounding->set_theta(Slice<const ProfilePoint>{theta, 3});

// Vapor: moist near the ground, drying out with height.
ProfilePoint vapor[] = {
    {    0.0f, 0.014f},   // 14 g/kg - moist boundary layer
    { 3000.0f, 0.006f},
    {10000.0f, 0.0005f},
};
sounding->set_vapor(Slice<const ProfilePoint>{vapor, 3});

// Wind: veering and strengthening with height (east, north).
WindPoint wind[] = {
    {    0.0f, -8.0f, -6.0f},
    { 6000.0f,  8.0f,  1.0f},
    {12000.0f, 12.0f,  2.0f},
};
sounding->set_wind(Slice<const WindPoint>{wind, 3});

sounding->set_surface_pressure(100000.0f);  // ~1000 hPa, sea-level-ish

You do not have to set all four. Anything you leave alone keeps the starting value of whichever constructor you began from. A common pattern is to take sounding_convective() and only tweak the wind, or only moisten the low levels.

Tip: Surface pressure near sea level is about 101325 Pa (1013 hPa). Higher ground means lower surface pressure. If you are not modeling altitude on purpose, 100000.0f (1000 hPa) is a clean, standard choice.

Analyzing a sounding before you run

Because a sounding is pure data, you can ask it what kind of weather it will support without starting a simulation. This is how you tune an environment quickly.

SoundingReportData report = sounding->analyze();
std::printf("CAPE %.0f J/kg, 0-6km shear (%.1f, %.1f) m/s, 0-1km SRH %.0f\n",
            report.cape, report.shear_0_6km.x, report.shear_0_6km.y,
            report.srh_0_1km);

analyze() lifts a surface parcel through the profile and measures the storm ingredients: CAPE, CIN, the key levels (LCL, LFC, EL), bulk shear, storm motion, and helicity. Those numbers, and what counts as “a lot,” are the subject of the next two chapters (CAPE and the key levels, Wind shear and rotating storms).

A few helpers on the same handle:

  • analyze_below(top) - same analysis but cap the parcel’s climb at top meters (useful for shallow-domain checks). analyze() caps at 16 km.
  • bunkers_right_mover() - just the estimated supercell motion vector (m/s). Subtract it from your wind profile to keep a supercell parked in a fixed domain (see the recipes).
  • report_text(top) - a multi-line, plain-language summary you can log while tuning.

The sounding does two jobs

It helps to keep these separate in your head:

  1. The base state. When you create_sim, the sounding becomes the background atmosphere the whole domain starts in - the calm air that perturbations (seeds, feeds) are measured against. This is the “given” the Heat and Water chapters kept referring to.
  2. A brush. You can also hand a different sounding to a scenario as an air mass - a region painted with another atmosphere’s air (a cold pool, a moist tongue, a dry slot). More in Scenarios.

Takeaways

  • A sounding is the atmosphere by height: theta, vapor, and wind profiles plus surface pressure. It is your world’s starting air.
  • The Sounding handle is cheap and synchronous. Start from sounding_convective() for storms, sounding_quiet() for calm, or sounding_new() to build from scratch.
  • Author profiles with set_theta / set_vapor / set_wind / set_surface_pressure, as ascending (altitude, value) points.
  • analyze() tells you what a sounding will do before you run it - CAPE, shear, helicity, and the key levels. Tune here; it is instant.

Next: reading that report - CAPE, CIN, and the levels that decide if you get a storm at all.

Storm fuel: CAPE, CIN, and the key levels

When you call analyze() on a sounding (or probe() a live column), you get back a SoundingReportData. This page decodes the thermodynamic half of that report - the numbers that answer “will this air storm, and how hard?” The wind half (shear, helicity, storm motion) is the next chapter.

All of these come from one thought experiment: lift a parcel of surface air straight up and watch how it compares to its surroundings at each height. That is the story from Why air rises, now turned into numbers.

CAPE - the fuel gauge

CAPE stands for Convective Available Potential Energy. It is the total energy a rising parcel gains from buoyancy on its way up - the size of the storm’s fuel tank. Units are joules per kilogram (J/kg), and it is the field report.cape.

Bigger CAPE means the updraft can accelerate harder and reach higher, which means stronger storms, taller clouds, bigger hail, heavier rain.

ELI5: CAPE is how much “push up” the atmosphere has stored for a parcel that gets going. Small CAPE is a gentle nudge. Huge CAPE is a trampoline.

A rough scale to calibrate your soundings:

CAPE (J/kg)What to expect
0No convection possible. Parcel never becomes buoyant.
100 - 1000Weak - small showers, fair-weather cumulus.
1000 - 2500Moderate - healthy thunderstorms.
2500 - 4000Strong - severe storm potential.
4000+Extreme - explosive updrafts.

The engine’s sounding_convective() sits comfortably in the storm-producing range. If your custom sounding reports CAPE near zero, it will not storm no matter how hard you seed it - the fix is a warmer or (more often) moister low level, which raises CAPE.

CIN - the lid you must break

CIN stands for Convective Inhibition. It is the energy the parcel loses in the stable layer near the ground before it reaches the height where it becomes buoyant. It is the strength of the “cap” or “lid” holding the storm down. Units are J/kg; the field report.cin is reported as a positive magnitude (a bigger number = a stronger cap).

CIN is the barrier from the ball-in-a-dip analogy. Your seed (or feed, or air-mass boundary) has to supply enough lift to carry the parcel over it.

  • Low CIN (near 0) - almost no cap. Storms fire easily, even from a small seed. Can be too easy: everything erupts at once.
  • Moderate CIN - a cap that suppresses weak triggers but breaks under a strong one. This is the sweet spot: it lets pressure build so that when a storm goes, it goes big and isolated (the classic supercell setup).
  • High CIN - a strong cap. Even a good seed may fail to break through. If your storms fizzle immediately, high CIN is a prime suspect.

There is a real tension here: CAPE wants to be high (more fuel) and CIN wants to be low enough that your trigger can break it. High CAPE with a cap that never breaks produces nothing. Tuning a good storm environment is largely balancing these two.

The three key levels

As the parcel rises it passes three named heights, all reported in meters. Any of them can be NaN (not-a-number) if it does not exist for that sounding - for example, a parcel that never becomes buoyant has no LFC or EL. Always check for NaN before using them.

LCL - lifting condensation level (report.lcl)

The height where the rising parcel cools to saturation and cloud forms - literally cloud base. A low LCL means moist air and clouds that sit close to the ground; a high LCL means dry air and high, ragged cloud bases. Low LCL is one ingredient in tornado-friendly setups (the rotation has less distance to reach the ground).

LFC - level of free convection (report.lfc)

The height where the parcel finally becomes warmer than its surroundings and rises on its own, no more lift needed. This is the “over the lip of the dip” point - above it, the storm is self-sustaining. The gap between the surface and the LFC is the capped layer whose energy cost is CIN.

EL - equilibrium level (report.el)

High up, where the parcel has cooled back to match its surroundings and stops accelerating. This is roughly the storm top - the height of the flat anvil cloud. A high EL means a deep storm. It also tells you how tall your simulation domain needs to be: if your domain top is below the EL, you are clipping the storm.

Here is the vertical picture:

  EL   ~ 12 km  --- storm top / anvil; parcel stops rising
       |
       |         <- parcel buoyant here; energy gained = CAPE
  LFC  ~ 2 km   --- becomes buoyant on its own ("over the lip")
       |         <- capped layer; energy lost = CIN
  LCL  ~ 1 km   --- cloud base; vapor starts condensing
       |         <- parcel lifted by your seed, still not buoyant
 sfc     0 m    --- surface parcel starts here

Reading the report in code

SoundingReportData r = sounding->analyze();
if (!r.valid) { /* probe fell outside the domain; ignore */ }

std::printf("CAPE %.0f J/kg   CIN %.0f J/kg\n", r.cape, r.cin);
std::printf("cloud base (LCL) %.0f m\n", r.lcl);
if (!std::isnan(r.lfc)) std::printf("self-sustaining above (LFC) %.0f m\n", r.lfc);
if (!std::isnan(r.el))  std::printf("storm top (EL) %.0f m\n", r.el);

The same SoundingReportData shape comes back three ways, always meaning the same thing:

  • from analyze() on a sounding you authored (before running),
  • from probe(position) on a live simulation column (an Probed event) - the effective sounding right now at a spot, which changes as the storm modifies its own environment,
  • and its two headline composite indices, supercell_composite and significant_tornado, combine this thermodynamic fuel with the wind ingredients from the next chapter.

A tuning checklist

When an environment is not behaving, read the report in this order:

  1. CAPE near zero? No fuel. Moisten the low levels or warm the surface.
  2. CAPE fine but nothing fires? The cap (CIN) is too strong for your seed. Seed harder (bigger warm/moist amplitude), or soften the cap.
  3. Everything erupts instantly and messily? CIN is too low. Add a little cap so storms stay isolated.
  4. Storm looks chopped off at the top? Your domain top is below the EL. Raise the domain (taller vertical axis).

Takeaways

  • CAPE (J/kg) is the fuel: how much energy a rising parcel gains. Higher = stronger storms. Aim for ~1000+ to storm, ~2500+ for severe.
  • CIN (J/kg) is the cap: energy the parcel must be forced through first. Some CIN is good (isolated storms); too much means your seed cannot break it.
  • LCL / LFC / EL are cloud base, the self-sustaining height, and the storm top, in meters. They can be NaN - check before use. The EL also tells you how tall your domain must be.
  • The same report comes from analyze() (before) and probe() (live), so you can tune offline and monitor online.

Next: the wind ingredients that decide whether all this fuel builds a shower or a rotating supercell.

Wind shear and rotating storms

CAPE tells you how strong a storm can be. The wind ingredients tell you what shape it takes: a brief blob that rains itself out, or an organized, rotating supercell that lives for hours and spins up tornadoes. This page covers the wind half of the SoundingReportData - shear, helicity, storm motion, and the two composite indices - and how to use them.

Recap: shear organizes storms

From Wind: wind shear is the change of wind with height. Low shear lets a storm’s rain fall back into its own updraft and choke it (short-lived showers). High shear blows the rain and downdraft away from the updraft so the two do not fight, letting the storm stand up, breathe, and persist. High shear is the price of admission for organized severe weather.

The engine reports 0-6 km bulk shear as report.shear_0_6km, a vector in m/s: the wind at ~6 km minus the wind at the surface. Its magnitude is what matters most.

Vec2 s = report.shear_0_6km;
float shear_mag = std::sqrt(s.x*s.x + s.y*s.y);   // m/s

Rough guide to the magnitude:

0-6 km shear (m/s)Storm mode
< 10Weak - disorganized single cells (“popcorn”).
10 - 20Moderate - multicell clusters and lines.
20 - 30Strong - supercells become possible.
30+Very strong - classic supercell shear.

Helicity: shear that spins

Shear magnitude says the wind changes a lot. But to make a storm rotate, the wind also has to turn (veer) with height, not just speed up in a straight line. Turning wind gives the low-level air a horizontal spin, like a rolling pin lying on the ground. A storm’s updraft tilts that horizontal spin upright and stretches it into a vertical, rotating column - a mesocyclone, the rotating heart of a supercell.

The quantity that measures “how much spinning air the storm can ingest” is storm-relative helicity (SRH). Units are m²/s² (meters squared per second squared - it is an energy-like measure of streamwise spin). The engine reports two depths:

  • report.srh_0_1km - helicity in the lowest 1 km. This is the one tied to low-level rotation and tornado potential.
  • report.srh_0_3km - helicity in the lowest 3 km. Tied to overall supercell organization.

ELI5: Imagine air near the ground rolling forward like a pencil rolling on a desk. A strong updraft grabs that rolling pencil, stands it on end, and spins it fast - now the storm is turning. SRH counts how much rolling the storm has to grab.

Rough guide to srh_0_1km:

0-1 km SRH (m²/s²)Rotation potential
< 100Little low-level rotation.
100 - 200Supercells with rotation likely.
200 - 300Strong rotation; tornado-favorable.
300+Very high; significant-tornado territory.

Helicity is “storm-relative” - it depends on how fast the storm itself is moving through the wind field. Which brings us to storm motion.

Storm motion: where the storm goes (and how to park it)

A supercell does not simply drift with the average wind. Because of the way its rotating updraft interacts with the shear, it deviates - a right-moving supercell (in this wind setup) pulls to the right of the mean wind and moves slower. There is a well-known empirical estimate of this called the Bunkers storm motion, and the engine computes it for you:

  • report.storm_motion - the Bunkers right-mover motion vector (m/s).
  • sounding->bunkers_right_mover() - the same vector, if that is all you need.

This is enormously practical, because of the problem from Wind: a moving storm walks off your grid. The fix is to subtract the storm motion from your entire wind profile. That shifts you into a frame that moves with the storm - the storm sits still in the middle of your domain while the environment flows past it. You keep the same relative winds (so the same shear and helicity, so the same storm), you just stop it from translating.

// Park a supercell: subtract its predicted motion from every wind level.
Vec2 motion = sounding->bunkers_right_mover();
WindPoint wind[] = { /* your levels ... */ };
for (WindPoint& w : wind) {
    w.east  -= static_cast<float>(motion.x);
    w.north -= static_cast<float>(motion.y);
}
sounding->set_wind(Slice<const WindPoint>{wind, count});

The full version is in the recipes. If instead you want the storm to travel across a big map, skip this and let the adaptive grid chase it.

The composite indices: one-number screens

Combining CAPE, shear, and helicity by hand is fiddly, so meteorologists use composite parameters that fold the ingredients into a single number. The engine reports two, both dimensionless:

  • supercell_composite (SCP) - roughly, is this a supercell environment? Values around 1 and up indicate supercell-supportive conditions; higher is stronger.
  • significant_tornado (STP) - is this a significant tornado environment? Values around 1 and up flag setups capable of strong tornadoes.

Use these as quick screens while tuning: bump moisture, shear, or low-level turning until SCP and STP climb into the range you want, then look at the individual ingredients to see which lever moved.

std::printf("SCP %.1f  STP %.1f\n",
            report.supercell_composite, report.significant_tornado);

Think of them as difficulty dials for “how severe is this world,” not as physics inputs - the simulation runs off the profiles; the indices just summarize them.

Putting the ingredients together

A severe, rotating storm needs all of these at once. The recipe, in the engine’s own numbers:

IngredientReport fieldWant (for a supercell)
Fuelcape2000+ J/kg
A breakable capcinmodest, not huge
Deep shearshear_0_6km20+ m/s magnitude
Low-level spinsrh_0_1km150+ m²/s²
Screensupercell_composite≥ 1

Miss any one and you get something less: lots of CAPE but no shear gives a pulse storm that collapses on itself; lots of shear but no CAPE gives windy nothing. sounding_convective() is built to have all of them, which is why it is the recommended starting environment.

Takeaways

  • 0-6 km bulk shear (shear_0_6km, m/s) sets storm organization; ~20+ m/s magnitude enables supercells.
  • Storm-relative helicity (srh_0_1km / srh_0_3km, m²/s²) measures spinning air the storm can ingest; the 0-1 km value drives low-level rotation and tornado potential.
  • Bunkers storm motion (storm_motion / bunkers_right_mover()) predicts where a supercell goes; subtract it from the wind profile to park the storm in a fixed domain.
  • supercell_composite and significant_tornado are one-number screens combining the ingredients - handy difficulty dials while tuning.
  • A real supercell needs fuel and shear and helicity together; sounding_convective() supplies all three.

How the engine is shaped

You now understand the weather. Part III is the machine that simulates it. This first page is the mental model of how you talk to the engine - which matters a lot, because WeatherEngine is built around one firm rule: the simulation must never stall your game.

The problem it solves

A weather step is heavy. Advancing a storm one tick can take milliseconds - sometimes many. If your game called into the simulation and waited for it to finish, every heavy step would freeze the frame. That is unacceptable in a game running at 60 or 120 frames per second.

So WeatherEngine does not run on your game thread at all. It runs on its own background worker thread, and you talk to it by message-passing. You send it a command and immediately get on with your frame; later, you pick up the result whenever it is ready. Nothing you call ever blocks.

The actor pattern

This design is called an actor: one owner of the state, talked to only through messages.

   your game thread                         the worker thread
  ------------------                       -------------------
   client->advance(...)  --- command --->   (parks until a command
   client->grab_...(...) --- command --->    arrives, then runs the
                                             heavy step here)
   client->poll()        <-- event ------   posts results back
   client->poll()        <-- event ------
  • You send commands with methods on a SimClient: create_sim, advance, grab_presets, probe, and so on. Each one is non-blocking - it drops a message in a queue and returns right away.
  • The worker owns the actual simulation. It sleeps when the queue is empty and wakes up to run each command. Heavy work happens here, off your frame.
  • You poll for results with poll(), which hands back one SimEvent at a time, or an empty one if nothing is ready yet. You never wait; you just check what has arrived and move on.

The key property: there is no shared, locked state between the two threads. Data moves only as messages. That is what keeps the game thread free.

Sending a command: the request id

Every command method returns a uint64_t request id:

uint64_t id = client->advance(5, 5);
if (id == 0) {
    // The command queue was full - the worker is behind. Skip this
    // frame's command and try again next frame. This is normal
    // backpressure, not an error.
}
  • A non-zero id means the command was accepted and queued. The event that eventually answers it will carry the same id in event->seq(), so you can match responses to requests if you need to.
  • Zero means the request queue was full. The queue is bounded (it holds a few hundred commands), so this only happens if you fire commands far faster than the worker drains them. The right response is simply to skip and retry next frame - never to spin or block.

Receiving results: poll and drain

Results come back as events. Each frame you drain the event queue - poll repeatedly until it is empty - and act on whatever you find:

for (;;) {
    Box<SimEvent> event = client->poll();
    SimEventKind kind = event->kind();
    if (kind == SimEventKind::None) {
        break;  // queue empty - done for this frame
    }
    switch (kind) {
    case SimEventKind::Advanced:  /* read event->status()   */ break;
    case SimEventKind::Grabbed:   /* take the volume, render */ break;
    case SimEventKind::Regridded: /* read the regrid summary */ break;
    case SimEventKind::Error:     /* log error_message()     */ break;
    default: break;
    }
}

An event is a small tagged object. event->kind() tells you which variant it is; then you call the matching accessor. Here is the full set:

SimEventKindSent in answer toRead it with
None(queue was empty)nothing
Errorany failed commanderror_message()
SimReadycreate_sim / load_snapshotstatus()
Advancedadvancestatus()
Regriddeda regrid during advanceregrid_summary()
Grabbedgrab_fields / grab_presetstake_grab()
Probedprobeprobe_report()
Statusinstall_scenario / set_regridder / querystatus()
Valuesquery_max / query_precipitationvalues(), value_dims()

Nothing is ever pushed to you unsolicited. Every event answers a command you sent. If you did not ask for a grab, no grab arrives.

No exceptions, ever

WeatherEngine never throws. A bad configuration - an impossible grid, a physically unstable time step, a malformed snapshot - does not crash or abort. It comes back as an Error event with a human-readable message:

case SimEventKind::Error:
    std::printf("sim error: %s\n",
                std::string(event->error_message()).c_str());
    break;

This means you can experiment freely. The worst a wrong number does is produce an Error you can read and fix.

The threading budget

When you spawn the worker you also set how many CPU cores its heavy math may use:

Box<SimClient> client = new_sim_client(8);   // cap the sim at 8 cores
std::printf("using %u threads\n", client->worker_thread_count());

new_sim_client(worker_threads) builds a private thread pool of exactly that many threads, and all simulation work runs inside it. That is a hard cap: the weather can never grab more than those cores, so a heavy step cannot saturate the machine and starve your renderer.

  • Pass a number a few below your core count - e.g. 8 on a 12-thread CPU - to leave headroom for the game and the graphics engine.
  • Pass 0 to mean “every logical core” (use only if the sim has the machine to itself).
  • worker_thread_count() reports the resolved value (never 0).

The lifecycle at a glance

Everything in the rest of Part III fits this sequence:

  1. Describe the air - author a Sounding (Part II).
  2. Spawn the worker - new_sim_client(threads).
  3. Build the world - create_sim(config, axis, sounding, options), wait for SimReady. See Building a world.
  4. Start the weather - install_scenario(...) (seeds, feeds, air masses), optionally set_regridder(...). See Scenarios and the adaptive grid.
  5. Each frame - advance(...), grab_*(...), then drain poll(). Render the grabbed volumes. See Getting data back out.
  6. Shut down - drop the SimClient (or call shutdown()) to stop and join the worker.

Takeaways

  • The simulation runs on a background worker thread; you talk to it through a SimClient by message-passing. Nothing blocks your frame.
  • Commands are non-blocking and return a request id; 0 means the queue was full - skip and retry, it is normal backpressure.
  • Results are events you poll() and drain each frame; match on kind() and call the right accessor. Nothing arrives unsolicited.
  • No exceptions - failures are Error events you can read.
  • new_sim_client(threads) caps how many cores the weather can use; leave headroom for the game.

Next: building the actual world - the grid and the vertical axis.

Building a world: grid and vertical axis

To simulate the air, the engine chops your world into a 3D grid of cells and solves the physics cell by cell. create_sim builds that grid. You describe it with two structs: a GridConfig for the horizontal layout and an AxisConfig for the vertical layout. This page explains every field in plain terms and how to size them.

The trade-off you are making

One rule governs every choice here: smaller cells are more accurate but cost more. Halving the cell size roughly multiplies the work per step by 8 (twice as many cells in each of x, y, z) and forces smaller time steps, so the real cost climbs even faster. Your whole job when configuring a world is to spend fine cells only where they matter and stay coarse elsewhere. The adaptive grid is the engine’s main tool for that; here you set the stage.

A useful number: to actually resolve a convective updraft you want cells of roughly 100-250 m. Bigger than that and storms are mushy; much smaller and you pay dearly. The examples use 200 m finest cells, which is the sweet spot.

Horizontal layout: GridConfig

GridConfig config{};
config.dx_finest  = 200.0;                 // finest cell size, meters
config.levels     = 3;                      // quadtree refinement levels
config.brick_size = 8;                      // cells per brick edge
config.halo_width = 4;                      // halo ring, cells
config.base_bricks = VoxelDims{16, 16, 0};  // coarse bricks in x, y
config.origin      = Vec2{-51200.0, -51200.0};  // low corner, world meters

Field by field:

  • dx_finest - the size of the finest horizontal cell, in meters. This is your best-case resolution. 200 m is a good default.

  • levels - how many levels of refinement the grid can have. 1 means a uniform grid: every cell is dx_finest, everywhere, all the time. 2, 3, … allow the grid to be coarse by default and refine down toward dx_finest only where a storm is. Each extra level halves the cell size. This is a quadtree: horizontally, a coarse cell splits into 4 finer ones (2x2). The coarsest cell size is dx_finest * 2^(levels-1).

    With levels = 3 and dx_finest = 200, the coarsest cells are 200 * 4 = 800 m and the finest are 200 m. The calm air runs cheaply at 800 m; the storm gets the full 200 m.

  • brick_size - the grid is stored in bricks (small cubes of cells), and this is the cells-per-edge of a brick. 8 means each brick is 8x8x8 cells. Refinement happens a whole brick at a time, not a single cell. 8 is the standard value; you rarely change it.

  • halo_width - each brick keeps a ring of “ghost” cells copied from its neighbors so the solver can compute gradients at brick edges without reaching across memory. This is the width of that ring in cells. It must be at least 4 for the default cubic interpolation. Use 4 unless you know you need more.

  • base_bricks - how many bricks make up the coarsest level, in x and y (the z of this VoxelDims is unused - vertical layout is the axis’s job). This sets the domain’s width. The domain is base_bricks * brick_size * (coarsest cell size) meters on a side.

    base_bricks = {16, 16}, brick_size = 8, coarsest cell 800 m -> 16 * 8 * 800 = 102400 m = 102.4 km across. Matches the example’s origin of -51200 (half of 102.4 km), which centers the domain on the world origin.

  • origin - the world position of the domain’s low corner (the minimum x, minimum y), in meters. Set it to -(domain size)/2 on each axis to center the domain on (0,0), which is the usual choice.

Sizing the horizontal domain

Two quantities fall out of the fields above; keep them in mind:

  • Finest cell = dx_finest. Your resolution.
  • Domain width = base_bricks.x * brick_size * dx_finest * 2^(levels-1).

Make the domain a few times bigger than your storm so it has room to grow and move, but not so big you waste coarse cells on empty sky. If the storm will travel, either make the domain generous or park the storm (see shear).

Vertical layout: AxisConfig

Height needs different treatment from the horizontal. Near the ground, where the action is, you want thin layers; high up, where the air is smooth, thick layers are fine. So the vertical axis is stretched: layers start thin and grow with height.

AxisConfig axis{};
axis.k_levels   = 28;      // number of vertical layers
axis.dz_surface = 200.0;   // thickness of the lowest layer, meters
axis.dz_top     = 700.0;   // thickness of the top layer, meters
axis.max_growth = 0.10;    // max 10% thickness change per layer
  • k_levels - how many vertical layers. More layers = taller and/or finer domain, but more cost.
  • dz_surface - thickness of the lowest layer, meters. Thin here (100- 250 m) to resolve the boundary layer and cloud base well.
  • dz_top - thickness of the top layer, meters. Thicker (500-800 m) up where the air is smooth.
  • max_growth - the biggest fractional change in thickness from one layer to the next (e.g. 0.10 = at most 10% thicker each step up). This keeps the stretch gentle; abrupt jumps in layer thickness hurt accuracy.

The total domain height is the sum of all the (growing) layer thicknesses. With 28 layers stretching from 200 m to 700 m, that comes out to roughly 11-12 km - enough to contain a storm whose top (the EL from CAPE) is around 12 km.

Make sure your domain top clears the EL. If analyze() reports an EL of 12 km and your axis only reaches 9 km, the storm hits the ceiling and its top is clipped. Add layers or thicken them. The SimStatusData you get back in SimReady reports domain_top so you can check.

Custom vertical edges

If you need exact layer boundaries instead of a smooth stretch, use create_sim_edges and pass the explicit edge heights (strictly increasing, k_levels + 1 of them) in place of an AxisConfig. The stretched AxisConfig covers almost every case; reach for edges only when you have a specific vertical structure to match.

Putting it together: create_sim

With both structs and a sounding, build the world:

client->create_sim(config, axis, std::move(sounding), options);
Box<SimEvent> ready = pump_until(*client, SimEventKind::SimReady);
if (ready->kind() == SimEventKind::Error) {
    std::printf("bad config: %s\n",
                std::string(ready->error_message()).c_str());
    return;
}
SimStatusData s = ready->status();
std::printf("domain %.1f x %.1f km, top %.0f m, dt %.2f s\n",
            s.domain.x/1000, s.domain.y/1000, s.domain_top, s.dt);

create_sim takes ownership of the sounding (note the std::move) - it becomes the world’s base state on the worker. A SimReady event follows on success, carrying a SimStatusData snapshot (domain size, top, time step, finest cell size). A bad config comes back as an Error - never a crash.

The options argument (SimOptions) tunes the solver itself - time step, sound speed, boundaries. It has its own page, Options and keeping it stable; for now use default_sim_options() with the two acoustic tweaks the examples show.

A worked size: the quickstart domain

The smallest useful world, from quickstart.cpp:

config.dx_finest = 200.0;   config.levels = 1;   // uniform 200 m
config.brick_size = 8;      config.halo_width = 4;
config.base_bricks = VoxelDims{8, 8, 0};
config.origin = Vec2{-6400.0, -6400.0};
// -> 8 * 8 * 200 = 12800 m = 12.8 km box, centered on origin.

axis.k_levels = 24;  axis.dz_surface = 200.0;  axis.dz_top = 600.0;
axis.max_growth = 0.10;

A 12.8 km, uniform 200 m box, 24 layers tall. Small enough to step quickly while you learn, big enough to hold one storm.

Takeaways

  • The engine solves on a 3D grid; smaller cells cost much more, so refine only where the storm is.
  • GridConfig sets the horizontal grid: dx_finest (resolution), levels (1 = uniform, more = adaptive quadtree), brick_size (8), halo_width (>= 4), base_bricks and origin (domain size and position). Aim for ~200 m finest cells.
  • AxisConfig sets a stretched vertical axis: thin layers at the surface (dz_surface) growing to thick ones at the top (dz_top), gently (max_growth), over k_levels layers. Make the top clear the storm’s EL.
  • create_sim builds the world, takes the sounding, and answers with SimReady (or Error). Use create_sim_edges for exact vertical boundaries.

Next: putting weather into that world - scenarios.

Starting the weather: scenarios

A fresh simulation is just the calm base atmosphere from your sounding - nothing is happening. A scenario is how you make something happen. It is a list of edits to the air: a warm bubble here, a moisture source there, a pool of cold air over there. You author it on the game thread, hand it over with install_scenario, and the worker applies it.

There are three kinds of edit, in order of how often you reach for them:

  1. Seeds - one-shot pushes that kick off weather (the storm trigger).
  2. Feeds - persistent forcing that keeps pushing over time.
  3. Air masses - painting a region with a different sounding’s air.

The Scenario handle

Like a sounding, a scenario is cheap, synchronous, pure data. Build it, fill it, install it:

Box<Scenario> scenario = new_scenario();
scenario->add_seed_ellipsoid(Field::Theta, center, radii, 3.0f);
scenario->add_seed_ellipsoid(Field::Vapor, center, radii, 0.003f);
client->install_scenario(std::move(scenario));
pump_until(*client, SimEventKind::Status);   // wait for the install ack

install_scenario takes ownership (the std::move) and answers with a Status event when the worker has applied it. From Heat and Water, remember that all amplitudes are perturbations - offsets from the base sounding - so 3.0f on Theta means “3 K warmer than the background right here.”

Seeds: the one-shot trigger

A seed writes a perturbation into a region once, at install time, then leaves it alone. This is almost always how you start a storm: a small warm, moist bubble that provides the initial lift past the cap (convection).

Three shapes:

Ellipsoid - a soft blob (the usual one)

Vec3 center{0.0, 0.0, 1000.0};   // x=east, y=north, z=up (meters)
Vec3 radii {2000.0, 2000.0, 1000.0};   // half-widths in x, y, z
scenario->add_seed_ellipsoid(Field::Theta, center, radii, 3.0f);

A smooth ellipsoidal bump centered at center, with half-widths radii, peaking at amplitude in the middle and fading to zero at the edge. The classic storm trigger is a +3 K theta blob plus a +0.003 kg/kg vapor blob in the same spot, a couple of km wide, centered around 1 km up. Warm and moist is the point - the warmth lifts it, the extra vapor feeds the condensation once it rises.

Box - a soft-edged rectangular region

scenario->add_seed_box(Field::Vapor, min, max, /*softness=*/500.0, 0.002f);

A Vec3 min and max corner define the box; softness (meters) is how far the edge fades from full amplitude to zero, so you can make a crisp block or a gentle one. Good for a broad moist layer or a slab of cold air.

Path - a tube along a line

PathPoint pts[] = {
    {Vec3{-5000, 0, 500}, 800.0},   // position, tube radius (meters)
    {Vec3{ 5000, 0, 500}, 800.0},
};
scenario->add_seed_path(Field::Theta, Slice<const PathPoint>{pts, 2},
                        /*softness=*/300.0, -2.0f);

A tube of the given radius swept along a poly-line of PathPoints. Handy for a line of forcing - for instance a cold -2 K tube to mimic a gust front or an outflow boundary that fires a line of storms.

Seeds are idempotent. Installing the same seed again just re-writes the same bump. This matters after loading a snapshot (which clears the scenario) - reseeding a loaded grid is safe.

Feeds: persistent forcing over time

A feed keeps acting every step, within a time window, instead of writing once. Use it when you want the atmosphere continuously pushed - a warm updraft region that stays warm, a moisture source that keeps pumping vapor, a mountain-like heat source. Two effects, both on an ellipsoid region:

Nudge - hold a region toward a target

// Gently hold this blob 2 K warmer than ambient, forever.
scenario->add_feed_nudge_ellipsoid(
    Field::Theta, center, radii,
    /*target=*/2.0f, /*rate=*/0.01f, envelope_always());

Each step the field is pulled toward ambient + target at rate (per second - 0.01 means it closes about 1% of the gap each second, a soft hold). Nudging is stable and gentle; it maintains a condition rather than injecting unbounded energy.

Source - inject at a constant rate

// Pump vapor into this blob at a fixed rate while active.
scenario->add_feed_source_ellipsoid(
    Field::Vapor, center, radii,
    /*rate=*/0.0002f, envelope_always());

Adds rate field-units per second to the region regardless of its current value. More aggressive than a nudge - use it for genuine sources (a moisture plume) and watch that you do not run away.

The envelope: when a feed is active

Every feed carries an Envelope that switches it on and off smoothly over simulation time (seconds):

Envelope env{};
env.start   = 0.0;     // turn on at t = 0 s
env.end     = 600.0;   // turn off at t = 600 s
env.ramp_in = 30.0;    // fade in over the first 30 s
env.ramp_out= 60.0;    // fade out over the last 60 s

The ramps avoid a jolt from switching forcing on or off instantly (sudden changes make noise and can destabilize the solver). For a feed that is simply always on at full strength, pass envelope_always().

Seeds do not take an envelope - they are instantaneous by nature.

Air masses: painting with another sounding

The most powerful edit. An air mass stamps a whole region with the air from a different sounding - a different temperature, moisture, and optionally wind profile. This is how you build fronts and boundaries: a cold, dry pool crashing into warm moist air; a moist tongue feeding a dry environment; the classic ingredients that make storms fire along a line.

Box<Sounding> cold = sounding_quiet();   // a stable, cool air mass
// ... optionally tweak `cold` to be colder/drier ...

scenario->add_air_mass_box(
    *cold,
    Vec3{-50000, -50000, 0}, Vec3{0, 50000, 8000},  // the region
    /*softness=*/2000.0,
    /*with_winds=*/true,       // also stamp the cold sounding's wind
    /*nudge_rate=*/0.0f,       // 0 = initial condition only
    envelope_always());

Key parameters:

  • The sounding is the air you paint in. It is resolved against the live sim at install time - the worker samples that sounding into the region.
  • with_winds - stamp the source sounding’s wind too, or leave the existing wind. A cold pool usually brings its own (calm) wind.
  • nudge_rate - the crucial switch:
    • 0.0f paints the air once, as an initial condition, then lets the simulation evolve it freely (the front will move and mix).
    • > 0.0f also adds nudges that hold the region as that air over time (a persistent, replenished air mass - e.g. a cold source that keeps feeding outflow). The envelope then controls that hold’s timing.

There is an ellipsoid version too (add_air_mass_ellipsoid) with the same parameters and a radii instead of box corners.

clear() and rebuilding

scenario->clear() drops all seeds, feeds, and air masses so you can reuse the handle. In practice you usually build a fresh scenario each time you want to change the weather and install it; installing a new scenario applies its edits on top of the current sim state (seeds write their bumps, feeds begin acting).

Fire a single storm nowSeed an ellipsoid (warm + moist)

Takeaways

  • A Scenario is a list of edits to the air, authored cheaply on the game thread and applied by install_scenario (answered with Status).
  • Seeds write a perturbation once - the standard storm trigger is a small warm (Theta +3 K) and moist (Vapor +0.003) ellipsoid. Shapes: ellipsoid, box, path.
  • Feeds force a region over time within an Envelope - nudge to hold toward a target, source to inject at a rate. Use envelope_always() for always-on.
  • Air masses paint a region with a different sounding’s air - fronts, cold pools, moist tongues. nudge_rate = 0 paints once; > 0 also holds it.

Next: keeping fine resolution locked on the storm as it grows and moves - the adaptive grid.

Following the storm: the adaptive grid

A storm is tiny compared to the sky around it. Simulating your whole domain at storm-resolving 200 m cells would waste almost all of that work on empty, calm air. The regridder is the engine’s answer: it keeps fine cells only where the storm is and lets everywhere else stay coarse - and it moves that fine patch as the storm grows and drifts. This is what “adaptive resolution” means, and it is the feature that makes large, detailed weather affordable in a game.

You only need this if your GridConfig has levels > 1. With levels = 1 the grid is uniform and there is nothing to adapt.

The idea: refine where it matters, coarsen where it does not

Recall from Building a world that with levels > 1 the grid can sit at a coarse cell size and refine toward dx_finest in patches. The regridder decides, every so often, which patches deserve refinement:

  • Where the air is doing something interesting (a strong updraft, warm buoyant air, dense cloud, rotation), it splits coarse bricks into finer ones.
  • Where things have gone quiet, it merges fine bricks back into coarse ones to reclaim the cost.

The result is a grid that hugs the storm - fine over the updraft, coarse over the calm - and follows it around the domain. You run it periodically during advance, and it reports what it did.

Running it: advance’s second argument

The regridder runs inside advance, on a cadence you set:

// Advance 5 steps, and run the regridder every 5 steps (so once here).
client->advance(/*steps=*/5, /*regrid_every=*/5);
  • regrid_every = 0 disables regridding for that call (pure dynamics).
  • regrid_every = N runs the regridder every N base steps during the advance. Each run emits a Regridded event; the whole advance ends with one Advanced event.

Regridding is not free, so you do not do it every single step. Every few steps is plenty - the storm does not teleport between ticks.

Before it can run, you must install a configuration with set_regridder.

Configuring it: new_regridder and tiers

A regridder is a ladder of tiers. Each tier grants one more level of refinement, and says what signal justifies refining to that level. You build it, add tiers finest-last, and hand it over:

Box<Regridder> regridder = new_regridder(/*hold_rounds=*/3, /*min_age_rounds=*/3);

TierTrigger storm_birth{};        // tier 0 -> grants level 1
storm_birth.theta_above      = 0.8f;      // refine where |theta'| >= 0.8 K
storm_birth.w_above          = 1.5f;      // ...or |w| >= 1.5 m/s
storm_birth.condensate_above = 1.0e-4f;   // ...or cloud+rain >= 1e-4 kg/kg
storm_birth.zeta_above       = 1.0e30f;   // vorticity: effectively disabled
storm_birth.pad_bricks       = 1;         // refine 1 brick beyond the signal
storm_birth.max_bricks       = 320;       // cap this tier's fine bricks
regridder->add_tier(storm_birth);

TierTrigger storm_core{};         // tier 1 -> grants level 2 (finest)
storm_core.theta_above      = 1.0e30f;    // disabled
storm_core.w_above          = 5.0f;       // only strong updrafts
storm_core.condensate_above = 3.0e-3f;    // ...or heavy condensate
storm_core.zeta_above       = 5.0e-3f;    // ...or real rotation
storm_core.pad_bricks       = 1;
storm_core.max_bricks       = 256;
regridder->add_tier(storm_core);

client->set_regridder(std::move(regridder));
pump_until(*client, SimEventKind::Status);   // install ack

The tier ladder

  • The first tier you add grants level 1, the second grants level 2, and so on. So add them from coarsest trigger to finest. Level 0 (the base coarse grid) is always present and never capped.
  • A tier fires wherever any of its enabled signals crosses its threshold. A cell that meets a finer tier’s trigger gets refined all the way to that tier’s level.
  • The pattern above is the common one: a loose first tier catches the whole storm and its surroundings at medium resolution; a strict second tier drops the very finest cells only on the violent core (strong updraft, heavy condensate, real rotation).

TierTrigger signals

Each is a threshold; set a signal to a huge value (like 1e30f) to disable it. All map to concepts from Part I:

  • theta_above (K) - fire where the potential-temperature perturbation |theta'| is at least this. Marks warm buoyant air. See Heat.
  • w_above (m/s) - fire where the updraft/downdraft |w| is at least this. Marks active convection. This is the workhorse trigger.
  • condensate_above (kg/kg) - fire where cloud+rain water is at least this. Marks the visible storm body. See Water.
  • zeta_above (1/s) - fire where vorticity (local spin) is at least this. Vorticity is how fast the air is rotating; a threshold here refines the rotating core of a supercell - the mesocyclone from shear. Values around 5e-3 1/s mark real rotation.

pad_bricks and max_bricks

  • pad_bricks - refine this many bricks beyond where the signal actually is, as a margin. Because the storm moves between regrids, a pad of 1-2 bricks keeps it from outrunning its fine grid before the next run. Too small and the storm’s leading edge crosses onto coarse cells; too big and you waste fine cells on empty air.
  • max_bricks - a hard cap on how many fine bricks this tier may use (0 = uncapped). This is your cost ceiling: it bounds the worst-case work per step even if the storm grows huge. If the demand exceeds the cap, the regridder demotes the least-important regions to stay under budget.

Hysteresis: hold_rounds and min_age_rounds

If the grid refined and coarsened the instant a signal wobbled across a threshold, it would “flicker” - constantly splitting and merging the same bricks, which is wasteful and can jitter the solution. new_regridder takes two hysteresis counts (in regrid rounds) to damp this:

  • hold_rounds - after a brick’s signal drops below threshold, it must stay quiet this many rounds before it is allowed to coarsen. Prevents a briefly-calm patch from de-refining and then immediately needing to refine again.
  • min_age_rounds - a freshly refined brick must live at least this many rounds before it may coarsen. Prevents split-then-immediately-merge churn.

3 and 3 (as in the example) are sensible starting values. Raise them if you see the grid thrashing; lower them if the grid is too slow to release resolution behind a fast-moving storm.

Reading Regridded: did it keep up?

Each regrid emits a Regridded event with a RegridSummary. The field to watch is min_margin:

case SimEventKind::Regridded: {
    RegridSummary r = event->regrid_summary();
    if (r.min_margin == 0) {
        std::printf("warning: storm reached the edge of its fine grid!\n");
    }
    break;
}
  • min_margin - the distance (in finest bricks) from the nearest signal to the boundary of its fine region. It is your early-warning gauge:
    • -1 - nothing triggered at all (no storm, or thresholds too high).
    • large positive - comfortable; the fine grid surrounds the storm with room to spare.
    • small positive - the storm is nearing the edge of its fine patch.
    • 0 - failure: the storm has reached (and is spilling past) its fine grid. Its leading edge is now on coarse cells and detail is being lost. Fix by increasing pad_bricks, regridding more often (smaller regrid_every), or - if it keeps happening because the storm is fast - parking the storm.

The summary also reports splits (leaves refined), merges (leaves coarsened), and demoted_regions (patches the max_bricks budget forced back to coarse). Rising demoted_regions means you are hitting your cost cap

  • raise max_bricks if you can afford it, or accept the coarser edges.

When you do not need a regridder

  • Uniform grid (levels = 1): there is nothing to adapt; do not set one.
  • Small fixed domains where you can afford fine cells everywhere: simpler to run uniform.
  • Prototyping: get the storm right first with regrid_every = 0, then add adaptation for performance.

Takeaways

  • The regridder keeps fine cells on the storm and coarse cells elsewhere, and follows the storm - the core of affordable large-domain weather. Only relevant when levels > 1.
  • Configure a ladder of tiers with new_regridder + add_tier (finest-last). Each tier grants one level and fires on any enabled signal: theta_above, w_above, condensate_above, zeta_above.
  • pad_bricks gives the moving storm margin; max_bricks caps cost.
  • Hysteresis (hold_rounds, min_age_rounds) stops the grid from flickering; 3/3 is a good start.
  • Run it via advance(steps, regrid_every); watch min_margin in the Regridded event - 0 means the storm outran its fine grid.

Next: pulling the simulated weather out to render and inspect it.

Getting data back out

A running simulation is useless until you can see it. This page covers every way to read the weather back: grabs (dense 3D volumes for the renderer), probes (a full weather report for one column), and queries (cheap scalars and the precipitation map). All of them come back as events you drain from poll().

Pick the lightest tool that answers your question. A grab copies a whole volume every frame; a query copies a handful of numbers. Do not grab when a query will do.

Grabs: dense voxel volumes for rendering

A grab samples the simulation onto a regular 3D voxel box and hands you the raw floats, ready to upload as a 3D texture. This is how you render clouds and rain. Two flavors:

  • grab_presets - render-ready derived channels. Prefer this for visuals.
  • grab_fields - the raw prognostic fields (Theta, Vapor, W, …). Use for analysis, debug views, or custom shading.

The ViewBox: what window to sample

Both grabs take a ViewBox describing the horizontal window and the output resolution:

ViewBox view{};
view.min = Vec2{-51200.0, -51200.0};      // low corner, world meters
view.max = Vec2{ 51200.0,  51200.0};      // high corner, world meters
view.resolution = VoxelDims{128, 128, 48};  // output voxels x, y, z
  • min / max are the horizontal world box to sample (they need not match the whole domain - zoom in on the storm if you like).
  • resolution is the output voxel count. The z voxels are uniform slices over the full domain height, regardless of the stretched simulation axis
    • so the grab is a plain box you can drop straight into a 3D texture.

Note the grab resolution is independent of the simulation grid. You can render a 128x128x48 texture from a domain with millions of fine cells, or sample a small area at high resolution. Build the ViewBox once and reuse it every frame.

The preset channels

grab_presets takes a list of ChannelPreset values and returns one channel per preset, in order:

ChannelPresetWhat it isUse for
Cloudcloud-water density proxyrendering cloud
Rainrain-water density proxyrendering rain / precipitation shafts
Condensatecloud + rain togethera single “all visible water” volume
Temperaturephysical temperature, Kthermal/debug shading
 
ChannelPreset presets[] = {ChannelPreset::Cloud, ChannelPreset::Rain};
client->grab_presets(view, Slice<const ChannelPreset>{presets, 2});

Receiving a grab and the recycle loop

A grab answers with a Grabbed event carrying a ViewGrab buffer. You move it out with take_grab(), read its channels, then recycle the spent buffer so the next grab of the same shape reuses the allocation:

case SimEventKind::Grabbed: {
    Box<ViewGrab> grab = event->take_grab();

    VoxelDims d = grab->dims();                 // voxel counts
    Vec3 origin = grab->origin();               // world corner of the volume
    Vec3 vsize  = grab->voxel_size();           // world size of one voxel
    double when = grab->grab_time();            // sim time it was sampled

    for (uint32_t c = 0; c < grab->channel_count(); ++c) {
        Slice<const float> ch = grab->channel(c);  // flat volume, nx*ny*nz
        // ... upload ch.data(), ch.size() as a 3D texture slice ...
    }

    client->recycle(std::move(grab));   // hand the buffer back - no per-frame alloc
    break;
}

Two important details:

  • Channel layout is z-slice major: the flat array is nx*ny*nz floats, with x varying fastest, then y, then z. Index (x,y,z) is at x + y*nx + z*nx*ny.
  • Always recycle the buffer after uploading. The worker keeps a free list of buffers; recycling one lets the next same-shaped grab reuse it instead of allocating. In a per-frame loop this is the difference between zero and constant allocation churn.

Metadata to place the volume in the world

origin() and voxel_size() tell your renderer exactly where the volume sits and how big each voxel is in world meters, so you can position the 3D texture correctly. grab_time() is the simulation time (seconds) the grab was sampled - useful because the grab you receive this frame may be from a step or two ago (the worker runs ahead of your polling).

Probes: a full report for one column

A probe lifts a parcel from one world position and returns the same SoundingReportData you met in CAPE and shear - but for the live atmosphere, right now, at that spot:

client->probe(Vec2{0.0, 0.0});
// ... later, draining events ...
case SimEventKind::Probed: {
    SoundingReportData r = event->probe_report();
    if (r.valid) {
        std::printf("live CAPE %.0f, CIN %.0f, LCL %.0f m\n",
                    r.cape, r.cin, r.lcl);
    }
    break;
}

This is the tool for gameplay and telemetry that needs meteorological meaning rather than raw fields: show the player a “storm strength” readout, decide when conditions are tornado-favorable, trigger an event when live CAPE at a location crosses a threshold. valid is false if the position fell outside the domain. Because the storm modifies its own environment, a live probe drifts from the original sounding as the run goes on - that is expected and useful.

Queries: cheap scalars and the rain map

When you want a single number, not a volume, use a query. These are the cheapest reads.

query - a status snapshot

Returns a Status event with SimStatusData: current sim time, dt, step_count, domain size, domain_top, dx_finest. Use it to keep a HUD in sync without advancing.

query_max - domain-wide peak of fields

Returns a Values event with one value per requested field, in order - the maximum |value| of each over the whole domain:

Field fields[] = {Field::W, Field::Cloud, Field::Rain};
client->query_max(Slice<const Field>{fields, 3});
// ... draining ...
case SimEventKind::Values: {
    Slice<const float> v = event->values();
    std::printf("peak updraft %.1f m/s, peak cloud %.2e kg/kg\n", v[0], v[1]);
    break;
}

Perfect for “how strong is the storm right now” checks - peak updraft (W), peak condensate - without copying any volume. For query_max, the shape value_dims() is {n, 1, 1}.

query_precipitation - the accumulated rain map

Returns a Values event holding the surface precipitation map in millimeters - how much rain has accumulated at each base grid cell since the sim began. Here value_dims() is {nx, ny, 1}, the base-cell counts:

client->query_precipitation();
// ... draining ...
case SimEventKind::Values: {
    VoxelDims d = event->value_dims();          // {nx, ny, 1}
    Slice<const float> p = event->values();      // mm, row-major nx*ny
    float peak = 0.0f;
    for (size_t i = 0; i < p.size(); ++i) peak = std::max(peak, p[i]);
    std::printf("rain map %u x %u, peak %.1f mm\n", d.x, d.y, peak);
    break;
}

Use it to drive ground wetness, puddles, flooding, or a rainfall overlay.

Saving and restoring: snapshots

You can serialize the entire simulation to bytes and restore it later - for save games, deterministic replays, or rewinding:

  • save_snapshot() answers with a Snapshot event; read the bytes with snapshot_bytes() and store them however you like (copy them out before the event drops).
  • load_snapshot(bytes) rebuilds the sim from those bytes and answers with SimReady (or Error on a malformed buffer).

A snapshot captures the grid, fields, refinement, precipitation, and time - but not the installed scenario or regridder. After loading, reinstall your scenario and regridder if the run still needs them (reseeding is safe - seeds are idempotent, from Scenarios).

Next: the solver knobs, and how to keep a simulation stable.

Options and keeping it stable

create_sim takes a SimOptions struct - the solver’s tuning knobs. You get a validated set from default_sim_options() and override the few you need. Most fields you can leave alone. This page explains the ones that matter, with a focus on the one thing that trips people up: keeping the simulation stable so a bad number does not blow up (or, here, come back as an Error).

Start every time from the defaults:

SimOptions options = default_sim_options();
// ... override a couple of fields ...
client->create_sim(config, axis, std::move(sounding), options);

Why stability is a thing at all

The engine advances the weather in small time steps. There is a hard limit on how big each step can be relative to the cell size: if a step is too big for the cells, the numbers explode. This is a universal fact of grid-based physics (the “CFL condition” - think of it as “a wave must not jump more than one cell per step”). WeatherEngine checks this for you and, if you violate it, returns an Error event instead of running an unstable sim. So stability tuning here is really about staying under that limit while stepping as fast as you can.

There are two waves to worry about: the slow weather itself, and fast sound waves. Sound is the awkward one, because it moves far faster than any storm, which would force absurdly tiny steps. The engine uses a trick for it.

The time step: dt_per_meter

The base time step is not set directly; it scales with your finest cell size:

  • dt_per_meter - seconds of time step per meter of finest cell, s/m. The actual dt is roughly dt_per_meter * dx_finest. The default is tuned so the weather CFL is satisfied at normal cell sizes. Bigger = faster but closer to the edge of stability; smaller = safer but slower.

You rarely change this. The SimReady status reports the resulting dt in seconds so you can see what you got.

The sound-wave trick: sound_speed and acoustic_substeps

Real sound travels at ~340 m/s. Resolving it directly would force a tiny time step. The engine instead uses a slower artificial sound speed and takes several cheap acoustic substeps within each weather step, which keeps the pressure field consistent without crippling the step size.

  • sound_speed - the artificial sound speed, m/s. Lower means a bigger stable step (cheaper), but it must stay above the fastest air motion in your storm or the pressure solve misbehaves. The examples use 120 m/s - comfortably above storm updrafts (tens of m/s) yet far below real sound.
  • acoustic_substeps - how many acoustic substeps per weather step. More substeps let you use a lower sound_speed or larger cells while staying stable. The examples use 7.
  • off_centering - a small forward-weighting (~0.2) that damps acoustic noise. Leave at the default unless you see pressure ringing.

The one override you will almost always make

The default sound_speed/acoustic_substeps are tuned for a particular cell size. At the example’s 200 m cells the acoustic CFL is a touch tight, so the examples loosen it exactly like this:

SimOptions options = default_sim_options();
options.sound_speed = 120.0f;      // slower artificial sound
options.acoustic_substeps = 7;     // enough substeps to stay stable

If create_sim returns an Error mentioning an acoustic CFL violation, this pair is the fix: lower sound_speed and/or raise acoustic_substeps until it is accepted. (Internally the acoustic CFL must stay at or below 0.35; you do not compute it - you just nudge these two until the config is accepted.)

Mixing and reference density

  • diffusivity (m²/s) - how strongly the solver smooths small-scale noise, a stand-in for turbulent mixing. Higher = smoother, more diffuse storms; lower = sharper but noisier. Default is a good balance.
  • air_density (kg/m³) - a reference air density (~1.2 at sea level). Leave at default unless you are matching a specific setup.

Advection: how stuff is carried by the wind

advection picks how the solver moves quantities along with the flow (the interpolation used when tracing where each cell’s air came from):

AdvectionSchemeCharacterCost
Linearcheap, but smears/diffuses sharp featureslowest
VelocityCubicsharp winds, smooth scalars - the defaultmedium
MonotoneCubicsharpest everywhere, no overshoothighest

Stick with the default VelocityCubic unless you have a reason. Drop to Linear to save time when you do not care about crisp cloud edges; go to MonotoneCubic for the cleanest visuals at more cost.

  • skip_quiescent - a performance option: copy undisturbed (“quiet”) bricks straight through instead of advecting them. Safe speedup for domains with lots of calm air; leave it on if the default enables it.

The boundaries: sponges and lids

Your domain has edges, and weather hitting an edge would reflect back inward and corrupt the solution - like waves bouncing off the walls of a bathtub. Sponge layers are soft zones near the edges that gently absorb outgoing disturbances so they leave cleanly.

  • side_sponge - width, in base cells, of the absorbing zone on the x and y sides (a VoxelDims; 0 on an axis disables it there). Keep some side sponge if storms might approach the horizontal edges.
  • top_sponge - width, in cells, of the absorbing zone at the domain top. Important: a storm’s updraft slams into the top, and without a top sponge (or a lid) that energy reflects down. 0 disables it.
  • sponge_strength - how hard the outermost sponge layer damps. Too weak leaks reflections; too strong can reflect off the sponge itself. Default is balanced.
  • rigid_lid - instead of (or with) a top sponge, treat the domain top as a solid ceiling (zero vertical wind there each substep). Realistic when your top sits in the stable stratosphere, which naturally caps storms. Combine a modest top_sponge with the storm top clearing the EL (from Building a world) for clean tops.

If storm tops look like they are bouncing or piling unnaturally, revisit top_sponge, rigid_lid, and whether your domain is tall enough.

Parallelism

  • parallel - fan each solver pass out over the worker’s thread pool. This is a pure speed switch: the result is bitwise-identical whether on or off, so turning it on never changes the weather, only how fast it steps. Leave it on for real runs. (Recall the pool size is capped by new_sim_client, from How the engine is shaped.)

A sensible starting SimOptions

For a 200 m domain, this is all most projects touch:

SimOptions options = default_sim_options();
options.sound_speed = 120.0f;     // loosen acoustic CFL for 200 m cells
options.acoustic_substeps = 7;
// everything else: keep the validated defaults.

Then, only if you hit a problem:

  • Error about acoustic CFL -> lower sound_speed, raise acoustic_substeps.
  • Storm tops reflecting -> raise top_sponge or set rigid_lid, check domain height vs. EL.
  • Storms too noisy -> raise diffusivity or use MonotoneCubic.
  • Too slow -> ensure parallel is on, consider Linear advection and skip_quiescent, or coarsen the grid.

Takeaways

  • Always start from default_sim_options() and override sparingly.
  • Grid physics has a hard stability limit; break it and create_sim returns an Error, not a crash. The usual fix for a 200 m domain is sound_speed = 120, acoustic_substeps = 7.
  • sound_speed / acoustic_substeps trade step size against cost via the artificial-sound trick; dt_per_meter scales the base step with cell size.
  • advection picks sharpness vs. cost (default VelocityCubic); diffusivity smooths noise.
  • Sponges (side_sponge, top_sponge, sponge_strength) and rigid_lid stop weather reflecting off the domain edges - mind the top.
  • parallel is a free, bitwise-identical speedup.

Recipes

Ready-made setups you can adapt. Each says what weather it makes, which meteorology chapter it leans on, and the code that matters. 1. Smallest storm (start here)

Makes: one thunderstorm in a small box. Leans on: convection, scenarios.

A uniform 12.8 km box, a storm-ready sounding, one warm moist seed, no regridder.

Box<Sounding> sounding = sounding_convective();

GridConfig config{};
config.dx_finest = 200.0;  config.levels = 1;        // uniform 200 m
config.brick_size = 8;     config.halo_width = 4;
config.base_bricks = VoxelDims{8, 8, 0};
config.origin = Vec2{-6400.0, -6400.0};              // 12.8 km, centered

AxisConfig axis{};
axis.k_levels = 24;  axis.dz_surface = 200.0;
axis.dz_top = 600.0; axis.max_growth = 0.10;

// ... create_sim ...

Box<Scenario> scenario = new_scenario();
Vec3 center{0.0, 0.0, 1000.0};
Vec3 radii {2000.0, 2000.0, 1000.0};
scenario->add_seed_ellipsoid(Field::Theta, center, radii, 3.0f);   // +3 K
scenario->add_seed_ellipsoid(Field::Vapor, center, radii, 0.003f); // +3 g/kg
client->install_scenario(std::move(scenario));
pump_until(*client, SimEventKind::Status);

for (int i = 0; i < 60; ++i) {
    client->advance(10, 0);                            // no regrid
    pump_until(*client, SimEventKind::Advanced);
}

Tune: weak storm -> moister sounding or bigger vapor seed. Nothing fires -> bigger theta seed (the cap is holding it down).

2. A parked supercell

Makes: a long-lived, rotating storm that stays centered while the world flows past. Leans on: shear.

The trick is subtracting the Bunkers storm motion from the wind so the storm does not walk off the grid.

Box<Sounding> sounding = sounding_convective();   // already has good shear

// Park it: shift into the storm's own moving frame.
Vec2 motion = sounding->bunkers_right_mover();

// Re-author the wind profile minus that motion. (Use your own levels;
// these mirror the built-in convective profile.)
WindPoint wind[] = {
    {    0.0f, -8.0f, -6.0f},
    { 2000.0f, -2.0f, -1.0f},
    { 6000.0f,  8.0f,  1.0f},
    {12000.0f, 12.0f,  2.0f},
    {20000.0f, 12.0f,  2.0f},
};
for (WindPoint& w : wind) {
    w.east  -= static_cast<float>(motion.x);
    w.north -= static_cast<float>(motion.y);
}
sounding->set_wind(Slice<const WindPoint>{wind, 5});

// Confirm the environment still looks supercellular AFTER parking
// (shear and helicity are frame-independent, so they should be unchanged):
SoundingReportData r = sounding->analyze();
std::printf("CAPE %.0f  SRH0-1 %.0f  SCP %.1f\n",
            r.cape, r.srh_0_1km, r.supercell_composite);

Use a bigger domain (levels = 3, base_bricks {16,16}) and a regridder (recipe 4) so the fine grid tracks the storm’s internal structure even while it sits still overall.

3. Adaptive domain that follows a moving storm

Makes: a storm free to drift across a large map, with fine cells chasing it. Leans on: the adaptive grid.

Do not park the storm; instead give it a big multi-level domain and a regridder.

GridConfig config{};
config.dx_finest = 200.0;  config.levels = 3;        // 800 m coarse -> 200 m fine
config.brick_size = 8;     config.halo_width = 4;
config.base_bricks = VoxelDims{16, 16, 0};
config.origin = Vec2{-51200.0, -51200.0};            // 102.4 km, centered

// ... create_sim, install a seed near one side ...

Box<Regridder> regridder = new_regridder(3, 3);       // hysteresis
TierTrigger birth{};                                   // level 1: the whole storm
birth.theta_above = 0.8f;  birth.w_above = 1.5f;
birth.condensate_above = 1.0e-4f; birth.zeta_above = 1.0e30f;
birth.pad_bricks = 1; birth.max_bricks = 320;
regridder->add_tier(birth);
TierTrigger core{};                                    // level 2: the violent core
core.theta_above = 1.0e30f; core.w_above = 5.0f;
core.condensate_above = 3.0e-3f; core.zeta_above = 5.0e-3f;
core.pad_bricks = 1; core.max_bricks = 256;
regridder->add_tier(core);
client->set_regridder(std::move(regridder));
pump_until(*client, SimEventKind::Status);

// Each frame: regrid every 5 steps, and WATCH THE MARGIN.
for (int frame = 0; frame < 300; ++frame) {
    client->advance(5, 5);
    for (;;) {
        Box<SimEvent> e = client->poll();
        if (e->kind() == SimEventKind::None) break;
        if (e->kind() == SimEventKind::Regridded &&
            e->regrid_summary().min_margin == 0) {
            std::printf("storm reached its fine-grid edge - raise pad_bricks\n");
        }
    }
}

Tune: min_margin hitting 0 -> raise pad_bricks or regrid more often. Cost too high -> lower max_bricks (accepts coarser edges).

4. A front: cold air undercutting warm air

Makes: storms firing along a boundary, not from a single bubble. Leans on: air masses.

Paint a cold, stable air mass into part of a warm, unstable domain. Where the cold air undercuts the warm moist air, it lifts it and fires convection along the seam.

Box<Sounding> warm = sounding_convective();   // domain base state
// ... create_sim with `warm` ...

Box<Sounding> cold = sounding_quiet();        // a cool, stable brush
Box<Scenario> scenario = new_scenario();

// Stamp cold air over the western half, as an initial condition (nudge 0),
// so it is free to advance eastward and undercut the warm air.
scenario->add_air_mass_box(
    *cold,
    Vec3{-51200.0, -51200.0,     0.0},
    Vec3{     0.0,  51200.0,  6000.0},
    /*softness=*/3000.0,
    /*with_winds=*/true,
    /*nudge_rate=*/0.0f,          // release it; let the front move
    envelope_always());

// A gentle push to get storms going right at the seam.
scenario->add_seed_ellipsoid(Field::Theta, Vec3{500.0, 0.0, 800.0},
                             Vec3{1500.0, 4000.0, 700.0}, 2.0f);
client->install_scenario(std::move(scenario));

Tune: set nudge_rate > 0 to hold the cold pool as a persistent source (continuous outflow) instead of a one-time front.

5. Reading the storm each frame (rendering + telemetry)

Makes: nothing new - shows the read-out loop. Leans on: getting data out.

ViewBox view{};
view.min = Vec2{-51200.0, -51200.0};
view.max = Vec2{ 51200.0,  51200.0};
view.resolution = VoxelDims{128, 128, 48};
ChannelPreset presets[] = {ChannelPreset::Cloud, ChannelPreset::Rain};

for (int frame = 0; frame < 600; ++frame) {
    client->advance(5, 5);
    client->grab_presets(view, Slice<const ChannelPreset>{presets, 2});

    // Cheap telemetry a few times a second, not every frame:
    if (frame % 30 == 0) {
        Field peak[] = {Field::W};
        client->query_max(Slice<const Field>{peak, 1});
        client->probe(Vec2{0.0, 0.0});
    }

    for (;;) {
        Box<SimEvent> e = client->poll();
        switch (e->kind()) {
        case SimEventKind::None: goto next_frame;
        case SimEventKind::Grabbed: {
            Box<ViewGrab> g = e->take_grab();
            // upload g->channel(0) (cloud), g->channel(1) (rain) as 3D textures
            client->recycle(std::move(g));       // always recycle!
            break;
        }
        case SimEventKind::Values:
            std::printf("peak |w| = %.1f m/s\n", e->values()[0]);
            break;
        case SimEventKind::Probed:
            std::printf("live CAPE %.0f\n", e->probe_report().cape);
            break;
        default: break;
        }
    }
    next_frame:;
}

Glossary

Quick definitions, plain English. Meteorology terms and the engine terms that use them, together, so you can look up either. Links point to the chapter that explains the idea in full.

Weather terms

Advection - the carrying of something (heat, moisture, momentum) along by the wind. The engine’s AdvectionScheme option picks how this is computed. See Options.

Air mass - a large body of air with roughly uniform temperature and moisture. In the engine, a region painted with a different sounding’s air. See Scenarios.

Buoyancy - the tendency of lighter (warmer or moister) air to rise and heavier (cooler, drier) air to sink. The hot-air-balloon rule. See Convection.

Bunkers storm motion - an empirical estimate of how fast and which way a supercell moves (it deviates from the mean wind). Reported as storm_motion; subtract it from the wind to park a storm. See Shear.

CAPE (Convective Available Potential Energy, J/kg) - the storm’s fuel: total energy a rising parcel gains from buoyancy. Higher = stronger storms. See CAPE.

CIN (Convective Inhibition, J/kg) - the “cap”: energy a parcel must be forced through before it can rise freely. Reported as a positive magnitude. See CAPE.

Condensation - vapor turning into liquid droplets (cloud). Releases latent heat, which powers storms. See Moisture.

Conditional instability - the common storm-day state: stable to a dry parcel, but unstable once a moist parcel is lifted high enough to start condensing. Why a small seed can grow a storm. See Convection.

Convection - vertical air motion driven by buoyancy; the engine of clouds and storms. See Convection.

Downdraft - sinking air within a storm, dragged down by falling rain; spreads out as gusty outflow at the ground. See Convection.

EL (Equilibrium Level, m) - the height where a rising parcel cools back to match its surroundings and stops; roughly the storm top / anvil. Your domain should reach above it. See CAPE.

Helicity - see SRH.

Kelvin (K) - the temperature unit used throughout: Celsius + 273.15. 300 K is a warm surface value. See Temperature.

Latent heat - the hidden heat released when vapor condenses (or absorbed when water evaporates). The energy source that makes moist storms strong. See Moisture.

LCL (Lifting Condensation Level, m) - the height a rising surface parcel first saturates and cloud forms; cloud base. See CAPE.

LFC (Level of Free Convection, m) - the height above which a lifted parcel is buoyant and rises on its own, no more push needed. See CAPE.

Mesocyclone - the rotating updraft at the heart of a supercell. The engine can refine the grid on its spin via the zeta_above trigger. See Shear and the regridder.

Mixing ratio - mass of water per mass of dry air, kg/kg (or g/kg). How the engine measures vapor, cloud, and rain. 0.014 kg/kg is moist. See Moisture.

Outflow - the cool, gusty air that spreads out at the ground under a storm’s downdraft; can trigger new storms at its edge. See Convection.

Parcel - an imaginary small blob of air you track as it rises or sinks, used to reason about buoyancy and stability. See Convection.

Perturbation - the small departure of a field from the base sounding. The engine stores and seeds perturbations (e.g. a +3 K theta bump), not absolute values. Written with a prime, like theta’. See Temperature.

Potential temperature (theta, θ, K) - the temperature a parcel would have brought to a reference pressure; removes the cooling-with-height effect so you can compare air across altitudes. Constant with height = neutral, increasing = stable, decreasing = unstable. The Theta field. See Temperature.

Precipitation - rain reaching the ground. The engine accumulates it into a surface map in millimeters (query_precipitation). See Output.

Pressure (Pa) - the weight of the air above pressing down; falls with height. The engine tracks its small perturbation, the Pressure field. See The atmosphere.

Saturation - the most vapor air can hold at a given temperature; beyond it, vapor condenses. Warm air saturates at more vapor than cold air. See Moisture.

Shear (wind shear) - the change of wind with height. Organizes storms: low shear = brief showers, high shear = supercells. Reported as shear_0_6km. See Wind and Shear.

Sounding - the vertical profile of the atmosphere over one spot: theta, vapor, and wind by height. Your world’s starting air; the Sounding handle. See The sounding.

SRH (Storm-Relative Helicity, m²/s²) - a measure of the spinning air a storm can ingest; drives rotation and tornado potential. Reported as srh_0_1km and srh_0_3km. See Shear.

Stability - whether the atmosphere resists (stable) or encourages (unstable) vertical motion. Set by the theta profile. See Convection.

Stratosphere - the stable layer above the troposphere; acts as a lid that flattens storm tops into anvils. See The atmosphere.

Supercell - a long-lived, rotating thunderstorm; the type that makes big hail and tornadoes. Needs CAPE, shear, and helicity together. See Shear.

Tropopause / troposphere - the troposphere is the weather layer (ground to ~10-12 km); the tropopause is its top boundary. See The atmosphere.

Updraft - rising air within a storm; large positive W. The engine refines the grid on it via the w_above trigger. See Convection.

Vorticity (zeta, ζ, 1/s) - how fast the air is locally spinning. A threshold (zeta_above) refines the grid on rotating cores. See the regridder.

Engine terms

Actor - the design where the simulation owns its state and you talk to it only by messages (commands in, events out). See How the engine is shaped.

AxisConfig - the stretched vertical grid: layer count and thickness from surface to top. See Building a world.

Brick - a small cube of grid cells (e.g. 8x8x8); the unit of storage and refinement. See Building a world.

ChannelPreset - a render-ready derived channel for grab_presets (Cloud, Rain, Condensate, Temperature). See Output.

Envelope - a smooth on/off time window (with ramps) for a feed’s forcing; envelope_always() is always-on. See Scenarios.

Feed - persistent forcing over time within an envelope: nudge (hold toward a target) or source (inject at a rate). See Scenarios.

Field - a prognostic quantity the sim solves: Theta, Vapor, Cloud, Rain, U, V, W, Pressure. See The atmosphere.

Grab - a copy of the weather sampled onto a dense voxel box for rendering (grab_presets / grab_fields), delivered as a ViewGrab. See Output.

GridConfig - the horizontal grid: finest cell size, refinement levels, brick size, domain extent, origin. See Building a world.

Probe - sampling one live column into a full SoundingReportData at a world position. See Output.

Query - a cheap scalar read: query (status), query_max (domain peaks), query_precipitation (rain map). See Output.

Regridder - the adaptive-grid controller: a ladder of tiers that refine where the storm is and coarsen elsewhere. See the regridder.

Request id - the uint64_t a command returns; 0 means the queue was full (retry next frame). Matches to an event’s seq(). See How the engine is shaped.

Scenario - the set of edits that start weather: seeds, feeds, air masses. See Scenarios.

Seed - a one-shot perturbation written into a region (ellipsoid / box / path); the usual storm trigger. See Scenarios.

SimClient - your handle to the background worker: send commands, poll events. See How the engine is shaped.

SimEvent / SimEventKind - one polled response and its variant tag (Advanced, Grabbed, Regridded, Error, …). See How the engine is shaped.

SimOptions - the solver knobs (time step, sound speed, advection, sponges, …); start from default_sim_options(). See Options.

Snapshot - the serialized whole-sim state (save_snapshot / load_snapshot); does not include the scenario or regridder. See Output.

Sponge - an absorbing zone near a domain edge that stops weather reflecting back in. See Options.

Tier / TierTrigger - one rung of the regridder ladder and the thresholds (theta_above, w_above, condensate_above, zeta_above) that fire it. See the regridder.

ViewBox - the window a grab samples: horizontal world box plus output voxel resolution. See Output.

ViewGrab - the buffer a grab returns; read its channel(i) volumes, then recycle it. See Output.

Worker thread - the background thread that owns and runs the simulation, sized by new_sim_client. See How the engine is shaped.
